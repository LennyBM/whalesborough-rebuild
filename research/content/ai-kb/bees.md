# Bees at Whalesborough

**BLUF:** Whalesborough keeps approximately 15,000 Cornish Black Bees across 8 hives on the estate. They are the native dark European honeybee, slowly being restored after a century of imported Italian dominance. Honey is harvested in late summer (August-September). Live hive count appears on the sustainability dashboard.

Cornish Black Bees: native to the southwest of England, suited to the Cornish climate and forage cycle, more tolerant of cool wet weather than Italian honeybees. The estate sources queen bees from a specialised Cornish supplier; the colonies are managed with welfare-first practices.

What the bees pollinate: the 9,000 sunflowers, the 22,000 pumpkin seed plantings, the 50 acres of woodland edge, the wildflower meadows that surround the kitchen garden, and the apple orchard. Pollination is a key reason for the bees, not just honey.

Honey: harvest produces small batch jars sold in the estate shop (£8 for 250g, £14 for 500g) and used in The Weir kitchen and in the spa's signature treatments. Honey production is limited and seasonally scarce — when it sells out it sells out.

Honesty note: hive numbers fluctuate (winter losses, swarming). The dashboard reports the actual current live hive count, not a fixed marketing number. Last-reported date is visible; if it hasn't been updated by the beekeeper this week, it says so.
