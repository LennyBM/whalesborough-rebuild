### Article

**BLUF.** Whalesborough's two resident alpacas, Alberto and Alfonzo, live in a paddock at the upper end of the estate. They are not pets. They are not livestock in the working sense either. They are something quieter — a small, slow daily reminder that a farm is a place where animals set the pace.

[image: Wide first-light shot of the alpaca paddock. Hedge-line of Cornish blackthorn in the background, the two animals standing about three metres apart, both looking at the camera. Steam visible from their breath.]

The first thing to know about alpacas is that they are not affectionate in the way a dog is affectionate. I have been working with these two for almost four years now and I still, on most mornings, get the same low, sceptical hum when I open the gate. They will look at me. They will note my arrival. And then they will go back to whatever they were doing before, which is generally standing very still and considering the middle distance.

This is, I think, what people miss when they ask if they can stroke them.

#### Six in the morning

I get to the paddock just before six. In May the mist is sitting low across the valley and the dew is up over my boot caps before I reach the trough. I do the water first. Alpacas need clean water, not just any water — they will refuse a trough that has gone green even if they are thirsty, and they will refuse it silently, with a sort of disappointed dignity, until somebody notices.

*This is, I think, what people miss when they ask if they can stroke them.* [pull-quote]

I scrub the trough. I refill it. I put out their morning ration of pellet feed, which is small — alpacas have a slow, four-chambered stomach and forage best on the rough Cornish pasture for the rest of the day. They drift over once I am back at the gate. Alberto, the cinnamon one, always arrives first. Alfonzo, the cream, hangs back about a metre and watches.

This is their order. It has been their order since the day they arrived.

#### Why they are here

We brought Alberto and Alfonzo onto the estate in the summer of 2022, when we were thinking about how to make the upper fields feel — to use a word that gets thrown around too easily — *honest*. We had the wind turbine. We had the new market garden going in down at Neetfield. We had the beekeeper coming on board. What we did not have, in the upper paddock, was a reason for a guest to walk that particular hill.

[image: A child, perhaps five or six, watching Alberto from the other side of the paddock fence. Hands tucked into a yellow waterproof. No interaction — just the watching.]

Alpacas are good at giving you a reason to walk a hill. They are visible, they are unusual, and they will engage with you in a manner that is — and I want to be careful here — *companionable* rather than transactional. They are not asking for anything. They are not performing.

We also chose them because they are kind to ground. Alpacas have soft, padded feet rather than hooves. They do not poach the field. They do not over-graze. In a year in which we have lost a startling amount of British soil to compaction and run-off, this matters.

#### What guests do not expect

The single most common thing I get asked, by guests who have walked up from the cottages with a coffee in hand, is whether alpacas spit.

The answer is: occasionally, at each other, very rarely at humans, and almost never if you are not standing between them and their food. The spit is also not the green missile of cartoon. It is a fine, regrettable mist, and the alpaca who delivers it generally looks more embarrassed than the recipient.

[image: Close-up portrait of Alberto. Eyes amber, lashes absurdly long, a piece of hay caught on his lower jaw. Slightly amused expression.]

The second thing guests don't expect is the noise. Alpacas hum. It is a low, conversational hum, almost a question — *are you alright, then?* — and they direct it at each other constantly. If one wanders off, the other hums. If the wind picks up, both hum. It is one of the most peaceful sounds I know, and it is the reason I think these mornings are slower than they used to be.

The third thing — and this is the one I find guests carry away with them, often — is that alpacas have a particular tilt of the head when they are watching something they do not understand. A drone, a heron, a child running. The head goes up. The neck extends. The ears come forward. And the whole animal becomes a question, held still in the air, for as long as the unknown thing exists.

#### A working morning, continued

By half past six I have done the trough, the feed, and a quick check of their fleece — alpacas can get caught up in brambles, especially in spring when the new growth is sharp. Alberto has a small tangle near his shoulder which I work loose with my fingers while he watches the gate. Alfonzo, who is the more nervous of the two, stays back.

I do not rush this. Rushing an alpaca is a mistake.

[image: My hand, in the foreground, gently teasing a tangle of bramble from the cinnamon fleece. Alpaca's eye visible at the edge of frame, watching but not flinching.]

When the work is done I sit for a few minutes on the upturned milk crate by the fence post. I have a flask of coffee with me. The light is starting to climb the side of the cottage roofs down at the far end of the estate and I can hear the first of the kitchen staff arriving at *The Weir*. The alpacas, by now, are grazing.

This is the bit of the morning I don't tell visitors about, because it is the bit that matters. The work is fine. The work is the work. But the few minutes after the work — when the animals are settled, when nothing more is required of either of us — is when this paddock makes sense.

*The few minutes after the work — when the animals are settled, when nothing more is required of either of us — is when this paddock makes sense.* [pull-quote]

#### If you want to meet them

Alberto and Alfonzo are visible from the **estate map** [internal link: /estate-map] and from the upper walk that runs from the Trelowen lodges up past the woodland edge [internal link: /the-fifty-acres-we-planted]. We ask guests not to feed them — their stomachs are particular, and stray bread or fruit can genuinely make them ill — but you are very welcome to stand at the fence and watch.

Watching, with alpacas, is the whole of the thing.

Some of the children who come down from the cottages will stand at that fence for forty minutes. Their parents tell me, afterwards, with some surprise, that they didn't think their child would stand still for that long anywhere. Children, it turns out, recognise the slow animal. They settle into the same gear.

If you have walked up from the **W Club** [internal link: /the-w-club] or from **The Weir** [internal link: /the-weir-restaurant] and you have a few minutes spare on the way back, it is worth the detour.

The hum is the best part. The hum, and the long pause before the next one.

### Article

**BLUF.** Every washing machine at Whalesborough is fitted with an INDIKON commercial microfibre filter from Cleaner Seas Group, the first UK holiday resort to install them across all units. Each domestic wash can release up to 700,000 microfibres into wastewater. We are catching most, but not all, and the broader plastic problem in our oceans is not one filter installation can solve.

[image: Hero shot — INDIKON filter cartridge mounted on a Trelowen utility room wall, washing machine below, daylight from a small window. Honest, not styled.]

I want to start this piece by telling you what this is not.

This is not a brag. We installed the filters because they were the right thing to install, and we are pleased about it, but the moment a sustainability story becomes a brag, it stops being a story and starts being marketing. So let me try this differently. Let me tell you what we found when we opened a filter up.

#### What is in the cartridge

A used filter cartridge, after about three months of cottage washing, weighs roughly 180 grams more than a clean one. The material inside is a dense, felt-like mass. It is grey-beige. It smells, faintly, of warm dust. If you tease it apart with your fingers — and I have, in our utility room, while Tom from Cleaner Seas talked me through the methodology — you can see what it is made of.

It is hair. It is lint. It is, above all, fibre from synthetic clothing. Polyester from a fleece. Acrylic from a thermal layer. Nylon from a swimming costume. Polyamide from a sock.

[image: Macro shot of the spent filter media on a clean white tray, finger-teased apart. You can see distinct individual fibres in different colours — black, blue, red, beige.]

This is the stuff that, without the filter, would have gone down the drain, through the septic, into the watercourse, and — in our case, eventually — into the Atlantic.

The figure that gets quoted most often is 700,000 microfibres per wash. That number comes from a 2016 University of Plymouth study, replicated several times since, and it is the figure Cleaner Seas Group also work from. It varies. A wash of a brand-new fleece can shed two or three million. A wash of older, well-laundered cottons sheds a fraction. *700,000 is a middle-of-the-road average for synthetic textiles.* What is consistent, across every study I have read, is that the number is large.

#### Why we did this

In 2024 we read the Surfers Against Sewage water-quality report for Cornwall. We are five miles inland of Widemouth Bay [internal link: /local-area/widemouth-bay] and seven from Bude. Both are beaches we send guests to. Both are beaches our team surfs and swims and walks dogs on. The report was uncomfortable reading. Not the worst in the UK — but uncomfortable.

The water that leaves our site, after washing machines, does not go directly to the sea. It goes through our septic system, then to mains drainage, then through the regional treatment works at Bude. But the treatment works, like every other in the country, are not designed to filter out microfibres. They were designed for solids and bacteria. The microfibre problem post-dates the engineering of the system by about thirty years.

*The microfibre problem post-dates the engineering of the system by about thirty years.* [pull-quote]

So the choice was: ask the water board to retrofit a hundred treatment works in the next decade (unlikely), or fit the filters at source.

We chose source.

#### The install — what it looked like in practice

[image: Cleaner Seas technician — named, gloved — installing a filter unit on the back of a Beko washing machine in a Whalesborough cottage. The plumbing detail visible.]

INDIKON is the commercial-grade unit. It sits between the washing machine's outlet hose and the wall. The water from the machine passes through it on its way to the drain. The mesh inside catches fibres down to a stated 60 microns. That is finer than a human hair.

We have 30+ accommodation units. Each one needed a unit installed, a service door cut into the utility cupboard, and a quarterly service schedule set up. Cleaner Seas trained one of our maintenance team, James, to do the cartridge changes. Each cartridge — when full — is sealed in a labelled bag and goes to Cleaner Seas for analysis. The collected fibres are not, currently, recyclable. They are stored, weighed, and form part of the dataset that Cleaner Seas use for their ongoing research.

We are the first UK holiday resort to do this across all units. I am told. I cannot prove a negative — there might be a small B&B somewhere quietly doing the same and not telling anyone — but Cleaner Seas have confirmed to us, in writing, that they have no other commercial accommodation client at this scale in the UK.

[image: A labelled, sealed sample bag of spent filter material on a workbench, with the date and cottage number written in marker.]

#### What the filters do not catch

Here is the harder bit of the article.

The filters catch fibres released *during washing*. They do not catch fibres released into the air when clothing is worn and worn out — a category called "atmospheric microfibres" which, on some measures, exceeds the wash-water category by an order of magnitude. They do not catch tyre rubber, which is the largest single source of microplastic in marine environments globally. They do not catch the plastic that arrives at our coast from elsewhere — and a substantial proportion of the plastic on a Cornish beach is not Cornish in origin. It has come, in many cases, from container loss at sea, from rivers flowing into the Channel, from the Atlantic gyre.

In other words: the filters address one source, in one place, at one point in the cycle. They are not the answer to plastic in the ocean. They are an answer.

We say this because we have seen — and you have probably seen — sustainability pages that imply a single fix solves a planetary problem. That kind of writing produces what the trade now calls *greenhushing* or, worse, *greenwashing*. We are not interested in either. The filter is real. The catch is real. The wider problem is bigger than the filter.

#### What we have caught so far

The numbers are kept in the open. They live on **our impact dashboard** [internal link: /sustainability/our-impact] and they update weekly. As of the most recent reading:

- 412 wash cycles processed across the estate since installation
- Approximately 288 million microfibres prevented from entering wastewater (using the 700,000-per-wash midpoint, with the honest caveat that the actual figure for any individual wash will vary)
- 14 used cartridges sent for analysis at Cleaner Seas Group

We will publish a fuller annual report alongside the rest of the estate's environmental data. The methodology will be there — every claim with its working.

[image: The dashboard tile from the website, showing the live count, photographed on a phone screen held up against the Cornish coast in the background. A small visual joke about scale.]

#### Cornwall is the right place for this

It matters where this is happening. Cornwall has 433 miles of coastline. It has more Marine Conservation Zones than any other English county. It has the surf community, the swim community, the fishing community — all of which have noticed, in the last decade, what is on the beach and what is in the water.

When you can stand on the cliff path at Crackington Haven and see, on a clear day, the line where the river meets the sea, you start to understand that *the catchment is small enough to take personally.* What we do on our 450 acres is not separate from what arrives on the beach four miles away.

This is the part of the story that is, I think, beyond marketing. We installed the filters because we live here. Most of our team live here. Our guests come here because it is — still, mostly — a beautiful piece of coast.

If we are going to keep saying *beautiful* about it, we need to do the work.

#### What you can do, honestly

Two things.

If you bring synthetic outdoor clothing — and you should, this is the north Cornish coast — wash it as little as you can while it remains hygienic. Cold water. Shorter cycles. A Guppyfriend bag at home if you have one. The Cleaner Seas team will tell you the same.

And — this is the harder one — when you are buying replacement layers, look for natural fibres if the activity allows. Wool, hemp, organic cotton. There is a place for technical synthetics. Cornish winter cliff walking is one of them. There are many places where there isn't.

That is the sum of what we have learnt, what we have done, and what we still cannot do alone. The filter is on the wall. The cartridge is filling. We will keep telling you what's in it.

For more on the wider sustainability programme, including the wind turbine and the 50-acre woodland planting, see **our sustainability hub** [internal link: /sustainability] and the live **impact dashboard** [internal link: /sustainability/our-impact].

### Article

**BLUF.** The May welcome hamper that meets you on the kitchen island at Whalesborough contains nine items, sourced from six Cornish producers. The aim is the first hour of your stay should not require a supermarket trip — and that the producers behind every item are people we can name.

[image: The hamper open, lid lifted, on the Wormit-oak kitchen island in a Trelowen cottage. Soft afternoon light from the south-facing kitchen window. No styling props — this is the actual hamper as it leaves the stores room.]

There is a moment, when guests arrive after the long drive down from Bristol or Bath or further, when the front door swings open onto a quiet kitchen with the kettle already on the side and a hamper on the island. They put their bags down. They lift the lid. And — almost always — they take a slow breath.

That hamper has been my job, in some form, since 2023. We change it every month. We change it because Cornish food is a calendar before it is a list, and the calendar shifts under your feet faster than people realise.

Here is what is in the May 2026 version.

#### A small wheel of Whalesborough Cheese

[image: The cheese on a wooden board, wax paper folded back. The cut surface fresh and slightly damp from the morning's making.]

This is the easiest one to talk about because it is, almost literally, next door. Whalesborough Cheese is made on a farm two miles up the road by the Horrell family, who started in a single dairy room in 2007 and now produce a small, focused range of soft, semi-soft and washed-rind cheeses from their own herd's milk.

Our May hamper carries their *Miss Muffet* — a Caerphilly-style young hard cheese, four to six weeks aged, slightly crumbly, with a clean grass note. It cuts beautifully. It does not last long in our experience. Most cottages report it gone by the second evening.

#### A litre of Trewithen Dairy milk

Whole, in glass. Trewithen are the family dairy near Lostwithiel. They have been at it since 1994, and they have done what an increasing number of Cornish producers are doing — they have kept it small enough to know the herd, large enough to deliver to the cottages on time. The glass-bottle scheme is theirs. Empty bottles go back in the hamper basket on departure and get washed and refilled.

This is unsentimental. Glass is heavier. Glass breaks. We do it because the milk tastes better cold and because the bottles, returned, become bottles again. Plastic, once used, generally becomes a smaller piece of plastic.

#### A bunch of asparagus from Neetfield

[image: Just-cut asparagus on the kitchen counter, butt-ends still slightly muddy, photographed from above.]

This is the May-only item. From mid-April to about the third week of June, we have asparagus at Neetfield, our market garden at the lower end of the estate. By the time the spears are in the hamper, they have been out of the ground for under four hours. *You can taste it.* That is not a marketing claim — asparagus loses sugar rapidly after cutting, and the difference between four hours and four days is, frankly, a different vegetable.

After 21 June the asparagus stops. The plant needs to be left to fern out for the rest of the year so that it can build energy for next May. Hampers from July onwards carry runner beans, courgettes, and — by August — the first of the tomatoes.

#### A paper bag of Jersey Royal new potatoes

These are technically Jersey rather than Cornish, but they are the right potato for the month and we make no apology for crossing the water for them. Our supplier is **Robins Farm Shop** in Stratton, who have a long-standing arrangement with a Jersey grower and bring them across by the pallet rather than by the air freight tonne. They are in the hamper from late April to mid-June. After that we move to our own first-earlies from Neetfield.

A recipe for these is at the end of this piece.

#### A small loaf from Baker Tom's

[image: The loaf on a board, partly sliced, with a knife resting on the cut crust.]

This is the bread from Baker Tom's bakery in Truro. We had a long internal conversation about whether to bake our own bread on site (we don't have a bakery — we have a restaurant kitchen, which is not the same thing) or buy from a baker we trusted. We chose Tom's because they are a real bakery, with a real overnight prove, and because they are inside Cornwall. The loaf in the May hamper is their stoneground country.

#### A 250g jar of estate honey

Our 15,000 Cornish Black Bees produced 47 kilos of honey last summer. A small portion of it goes into the hampers in a hand-labelled jar with the year and the apiary number. The May 2025 honey is what is currently in the hampers; the 2026 harvest happens in September and goes into the autumn hampers from October.

We keep these jars deliberately small because — to be honest — we don't have a lot of them. The bees produce what they produce. *We have, in the past, run out by August.* Last summer two cottages received a substitute jar of Cornish honey from a neighbouring apiary, with a card explaining why. We will do that again rather than over-promise and bring honey down from Yorkshire to make up the numbers.

#### A parcel from Hoof to Block

[image: Brown butcher's paper, twine, the Hoof to Block stamp visible. Unwrapped to show the contents — in May, a piece of slow-cured back bacon.]

Hoof to Block are the Bude butcher we work with on every cottage hamper. They are a working butcher, not a chain, and what comes into the hamper depends on what they have that week. In May 2026 you have a quarter pound of their dry-cured back bacon. In other months it may be a piece of their slow-cured chorizo, or a guinea fowl breast, or — in autumn — a jar of their game terrine.

We use Hoof to Block because their meat is sourced from named Cornish farms within a 25-mile radius and they will tell you which farm. *That is not marketing language. It is in the paper bag, written on the parcel, by hand.* [pull-quote]

#### A few extra small things

There is always tea in the hamper — Tregothnan, the only commercially-grown tea in Britain, from the estate near Truro. There is always a small jar of preserve, currently the gooseberry from Boscastle Farmshop. There is a hand-written card naming each producer, which doubles as a directions sheet for the farms you might want to visit during your stay [internal link: /local-area/farm-shops].

There is no plastic in the hamper. The basket goes back at the end of the stay. The paper, the jars, and the bottles either come with you or go into the cottage's recycling.

#### What to do with the potatoes

This is for the new potatoes, while the asparagus is also in the fridge.

**Jersey Royals with estate butter, sea salt and chive**
Serves 4 as a side.

- 600g Jersey Royal new potatoes, scrubbed but not peeled
- 75g of the estate butter (or any good salted Cornish butter — the hamper carries a 250g block from Trewithen)
- A generous pinch of Cornish sea salt (Cornish Sea Salt Company — there's a small twist of it in the hamper card)
- A handful of fresh chives, finely snipped
- The juice of half a lemon

Boil the potatoes whole, gently, in well-salted water until just-tender — about 14 to 18 minutes depending on size. Drain. Return to the warm pan with the butter and let it melt over them, turning the pan rather than stirring. Sea salt, chives, lemon. Eat them with a hunk of the Whalesborough Cheese, the asparagus quickly steamed, and a glass of something cold.

That is the May hamper. Next month brings strawberries from Boscastle, the first of the cucumbers from Neetfield, and — if the bees have had a good June — the start of the new season's honey.

You can read more about the Neetfield Market Garden [internal link: /sustainability/neetfield-market-garden] and our **producer directory** [internal link: /larder/producers]. The hamper is part of every cottage stay — you don't need to add it.

**BLUF.** In the spring of 2023 we planted approximately 50,000 trees across 50 acres of formerly grazed upper pasture at Whalesborough. The mix was silver birch, sessile oak, hawthorn, blackthorn, hazel, rowan and field maple. Three years on, the survival rate is good but not uniform. The biodiversity audit shows real change. The story is not as tidy as the press release.

[image: Aerial wide of the woodland blocks, taken late April when the silver birch is just into leaf. The shapes are clearly geometric — this is human-planted woodland, not ancient.]

#### Why we did it

The fifty acres in question were grazing land. Sheep, mostly, then cattle, then sheep again. The soil was tired in a way that anyone who has spent time on a working farm will recognise — compacted from generations of hoof, low in organic matter, prone to run-off in the wet Cornish winters. The hedgerows around the parcels were intact and ancient but the interiors were biologically thin.

We had been talking about a planting scheme since 2020. The conversation always came back to the same question: was the right thing to do to plant trees, or to let the field do its own work and rewild more slowly?

In the end, we did some of both. About 38 of the 50 acres are tree-planted in defined blocks. The remaining 12 acres are managed as open ground — left to scrub, with occasional cutting to maintain a mosaic of habitats. The reasoning, after long conversations with the Cornwall Wildlife Trust and with our own ecologist, was that the south-west already has *fewer* woodland and *more* improved pasture than is ecologically healthy. A planted woodland, well chosen, would deliver more than a passively-rewilded grass field — at least on this soil, at this scale, in this timescale.

That last bit matters. We did not have a hundred years.

#### What we chose, and why

The species mix is roughly: 22% silver birch, 20% sessile oak, 18% hawthorn, 12% blackthorn, 10% hazel, 8% rowan, 6% field maple, with smaller proportions of dog rose, holly, and small numbers of wild service tree along the wet edges.

The reasoning was as follows.

[image: Close-up of a young silver birch sapling in tree-guard tube, late afternoon light, soft focus on the surrounding planting.]

**Silver birch** is the pioneer. It grows fast, it fixes the ground, and on Cornish acid soil it does what alder does in wetter country — it creates the conditions for the oaks behind it to mature. It is also short-lived, in tree terms. It will start to fall away in 50 to 80 years, leaving the slower trees the canopy.

**Sessile oak** is the long-game tree. It will not be a meaningful tree, in stature, for forty years. It will not be the dominant canopy here in our lifetimes. We planted it because if it is not in the ground now, it will not be there in 2100, and the sessile oak is the appropriate climax species for this geology.

**Hawthorn and blackthorn** are the workhorses. They establish quickly, they provide the dense thorny structure that the birds — particularly yellowhammer, linnet, and our resident dunnock — need for nesting. They flower early. They feed pollinators. They are also fundamentally Cornish hedge species and the planting feels visually correct because of them.

**Hazel** gives us a coppice option in twenty years. **Rowan** brings the autumn berry. **Field maple** is the one we are slightly worried about — it does not love the wet — but it adds canopy diversity.

#### What we did not plant

We did not plant Sitka spruce. We did not plant any non-native conifer. The Forestry Commission grants would have looked kinder if we had — sitka grows fast, the carbon-sequestration figures rocket on a 30-year horizon — but spruce plantations are biological deserts in this region and the carbon claim falls apart when you factor the soil disturbance, the lack of understorey, and the eventual clear-fell.

[pull-quote] *Sitka grows fast. The carbon-sequestration figures rocket on a 30-year horizon. But spruce plantations are biological deserts.*

We also did not plant beech. Beech is fashionable. Beech is also, in much of Cornwall, struggling — climate models suggest the south-west may become marginal for it within a generation. We left it out.

#### Year three: what survived

Three growing seasons in, the average survival rate across the 50 acres is approximately 84%.

This is — for context — about right. The Forestry Commission's expected range for a new woodland planting of this scale is 75–90%. We are comfortably in the middle of the band.

But the average hides a lot.

[image: Two side-by-side photos. Left: a thriving block of birch and hawthorn, well established, with grass cleared between the tubes. Right: a thinner block on the upper slope where the rabbit damage is visible — shorter saplings, more empty tubes, more weed growth.]

Block 4, on the south-facing lower edge, has done remarkably — maybe 94% survival, with the birch already over head height. Block 7, on the upper north-east slope where the wind comes off the moor, is sitting closer to 70%. The losses there are mostly hawthorn and rowan, both of which struggled in the dry summer of 2024.

We did not anticipate the dry summer of 2024 with the precision we would have liked. It is, however, the sort of summer we should now anticipate every three or four years.

#### What we got wrong

A short, honest list.

**Tree guards.** We used the standard biodegradable spiral guards. They have not biodegraded as quickly as the supplier suggested. We are now hand-collecting them where they have failed and they are unsightly across the lower edge of Block 2. We will switch to a different guard for the next planting, and we have built a tree-guard removal day into the autumn 2026 volunteer programme.

**Rabbit pressure.** We knew about the rabbits. We underestimated their numbers in the south-east corner. The deeper guards we used on the perimeter rows have worked. The shorter guards on the interior have not, in places. We have lost perhaps 200 saplings to rabbit ringing.

**Spacing.** We planted slightly too tightly in places. This is a long-term problem, not a short one — it will require thinning in fifteen years — but in retrospect we would have started with a marginally wider stand density.

**Watering plan.** We watered the saplings during the first summer. We did not have a contingency for the second summer, which turned out to be drier than the first. We have now installed an irrigation tap line down the central ride which will, if needed, allow targeted watering during establishment of any further planting.

[pull-quote] *We did not anticipate the dry summer of 2024 with the precision we would have liked. It is, however, the sort of summer we should now anticipate every three or four years.*

#### What changed

Here is the better news.

We have run two biodiversity audits — one in May 2023, before planting, and one in May 2026, just last week. Both audits were done in partnership with the Cornwall Wildlife Trust, using the same survey methodology and the same field walkers, for as good a like-for-like as we can manage.

The headline findings:

- **Breeding bird species** recorded across the 50 acres: up from 19 in 2023 to 31 in 2026. The new arrivals include garden warbler, lesser whitethroat, and — in one block — a confirmed breeding pair of bullfinch, which Cornwall Wildlife Trust were genuinely pleased about.
- **Butterfly species:** up from 7 to 14. The orange-tip is now common on the rides; the small copper has appeared for the first time since records began on this land.
- **Small mammal indicators** (live-trap survey under licence): bank vole numbers tripled. Dormouse: not yet, but the hazel is coming on, and dormouse colonisation typically lags planting by 8–12 years. We are not expecting them yet.
- **Plant species in the understorey:** up from 28 in the surveyed plots to 67. This is the change that surprised us most. The understorey has come in faster than we expected because we did not strim the ground between the saplings — we let it grow. That decision turned out to be the right one.

[image: A side-on photo of one of the rides through Block 4, showing dense understorey of foxglove, red campion, common dog violet — the new plant communities establishing themselves between the tubes.]

#### The shape of the next decade

We will keep the audit going annually. The full results will be published on **our impact dashboard** [internal link: /sustainability/our-impact] and discussed in the annual sustainability report [internal link: /sustainability/annual-report]. We will plant a further 8 acres in the winter of 2026/27, focused on filling out the existing blocks rather than starting new ones.

The 50 acres are not, yet, woodland. They are a young planting that, in 30 years, will be the start of a wood. In 80 years, it will be a wood worth its name. None of us will see the canopy at its mature height. We planted the trees anyway because the only sensible time to plant a tree is in advance of the future you would like to exist.

If you are staying with us, the woodland is open to walk through — the main ride starts at the upper gate beyond the Trelowen lodges [internal link: /stay/lodges/trelowen] and runs for just over a mile. It is a quiet walk. The birch is at head height now. The oak is still a child.

It is good walking weather, this week, for both.

**BLUF.** Most North Cornwall beaches restrict dogs between Easter and the end of September, but not all of them, not at all times, and not in all parts. The truth is more nuanced than the council signs make it look. Here is what we actually know, from walking ours here for the last decade.

[image: A black labrador and a terrier in the foreground, looking out across the wide flat sand of low-tide Sandymouth. Cliffs in the distance, blue October sky.]

If you walk dogs on the Cornish coast you will have had this conversation. You drive an hour. You park. You take the dog out of the boot. You look up at the council sign at the head of the path.

*No dogs on the beach between 10 May and 30 September.*

It is 12 May. It is 8.30 in the morning. The tide is out. There is no one there. And you do not, in that moment, know whether the sign means *all of the beach* or *only part of the beach*; whether it means *all of the day* or *only certain hours*; whether the dog you have on the lead, on the path, is in technical breach.

I have written this piece because I have had this conversation many times and I would like, if possible, to save you some of it.

#### How the rules actually work

There are two things going on, and they overlap in confusing ways.

The first is the **Public Spaces Protection Order** that each local authority (in our area, Cornwall Council) issues. These orders set dog restrictions on specific beaches between specified dates. The current orders generally run from **Easter Sunday to 30 September**, but the start date moves with Easter and the local authority can amend them.

The second is the **lifeguard season**, which is independent of the PSPO but tends to coincide with it. The lifeguarded sections of certain beaches are dog-free during lifeguard hours — typically 10am to 6pm in the high season.

[pull-quote] *The same beach can be dog-friendly at 8am and dog-restricted at 10.30am on exactly the same date.*

So the same beach can be dog-friendly at 8am, dog-restricted at 10.30am, dog-friendly again after 6pm, and entirely dog-friendly the next day if the lifeguards have packed up for the season. None of this is on most of the signs. All of it is on the council website if you know which page to look at.

What follows is the local picture, beach by beach, as we know it in May 2026.

#### Summerleaze Beach, Bude

Bude's main town beach. Wide, flat, sheltered behind the breakwater. **Dog restrictions Easter to 30 September**, between the breakwater and the lifeguard zone — but the north end, beyond the rocks towards Crooklets, is dog-friendly year-round.

[image: A low autumn-morning shot of Summerleaze with the breakwater catching low light, three dogs in the middle distance.]

In practice: in summer, walk it early. Before 9am the beach is essentially empty and the rules permit dogs across most of it before the lifeguards set up. In winter, the whole thing is yours. We walk it in October dawn light and it is one of the great walks in the West Country.

**Realistic tip:** the breakwater itself is open year-round and is a 700-metre walk on a stone path with the sea on both sides. Even on a peak summer afternoon it is dog-friendly and not crowded.

#### Crooklets Beach, Bude

Just north of Summerleaze, separated by a low headland. **Year-round dog access** on the northern half. The southern half (the bit nearest the cafés and the lifeguard hut) is restricted in season.

This is the one I send people to when they have arrived on a sunny May afternoon and don't want the seasonal grief. The walk along the southwest coast path between Crooklets and Northcott Mouth, north of here, is among the best dog walks in the area in any season.

#### Widemouth Bay

Big, wide, double-bay beach about three miles south of Bude. The headland in the middle splits it into two halves. **Restrictions apply to the southern half from Easter to 30 September.** The northern half (the part you reach by parking at the upper National Trust car park and walking down) is dog-friendly year-round.

[image: A dog in mid-leap on the firm wet sand of the northern half of Widemouth at low tide, wave coming in behind, late-May.]

In practice: park at the upper car park, walk the northern half. The southern half is the surf beach. If you have surfers in the family and dog-walkers in the family, split up — Widemouth is one of the few places where this works geographically.

**Realistic tip:** Widemouth low tide is a different beach to Widemouth high tide. The flat sand at low tide stretches for half a mile. The high tide pulls everyone up against the dunes. Check the tide tables before you set out — they live on **the estate map page** [internal link: /local-area/tides] which we keep up to date for guests.

#### Crackington Haven

Smaller cove, twelve miles south of Bude, dramatic black-cliffed headland. **Dog restrictions to the western half from Easter to 30 September.** The eastern half — closer to the stream — is dog-friendly year-round, and the coast path running south from the car park up to Pencarrow Point is one of the more spectacular dog walks in north Cornwall.

In practice: Crackington in summer is busy and parking is limited. Go midweek, or go at 4pm onwards when the day-trippers are leaving.

#### Sandymouth Bay

Five miles north of Bude. **Dog-friendly year-round.** This is, frankly, the answer to the question of where to take a wet retriever on a sunny July Tuesday.

[image: Wide low-angle of Sandymouth at low tide, dog in the foreground, the long curve of cliffs running north to Duckpool in the distance.]

National Trust car park (paid; free for NT members). A short, steep walk down the access path to the beach. Once you are there, you have well over a mile of flat sand at low tide, three sets of sea caves to explore at the south end, and — generally — a tenth of the people who are at Bude on the same day.

It is one of the best beaches we have. We send everyone here.

#### Northcott Mouth

Between Crooklets and Sandymouth. **Dog-friendly year-round.** Small parking, narrow lane in. The reward is a quiet cove with a fresh-water stream running across the sand, which dogs love, and a tidal pool at low water.

#### Welcombe Mouth and Marsland Mouth

Twenty minutes north on the Devon-Cornwall border. **Dog-friendly year-round.** These are the wild ones — long parking walks, no facilities, no café, no lifeguards, dramatic rocky beaches with the slate strata running out to sea. You will not have either of them to yourself in August but the ratio of dog to human is the right way round.

#### What to bring

A reusable water bottle and a collapsible bowl. The cliff-top walks are exposed and there is no fresh water on most of these beaches once the streams run dry in summer.

A towel that you don't mind getting beach-sand on, kept in the car. A second towel — for the dog. (There are dog-towel racks on the wall of every Whalesborough cottage [internal link: /stay/cottages/amenities].)

Poo bags. Several. There are bins at the main beaches but not at the wild ones. If you bring it, take it back to a bin. The bins at Sandymouth, Crackington and Bude are emptied daily in season but the bins at Northcott and Welcombe are not, and overflowing bins create a problem of their own.

A lead. Even at year-round beaches, you will encounter ground-nesting birds on the cliff path in spring (skylark, stonechat, meadow pipit). On the cliff path proper, *April to July, the dog goes on the lead.* That is not a council rule. That is the law (Countryside and Rights of Way Act, ground-nesting bird protection) and it is, more importantly, the right thing to do.

[pull-quote] *April to July, on the cliff path, the dog goes on the lead. That is not a council rule. It is the law, and it is the right thing.*

#### The slightly unfair bit

A note for first-timers.

The signs that catch most people out are the ones that say **NO DOGS** with no qualifying date. There are a handful of these in the area — usually on small private beaches or coves with a single landowner — and they mean what they say, all year, no exceptions. Trebarwith Strand has one in the high-tide section. Lansallos cove has one (much further south). Read every sign at the path head.

The other catch is the **end-of-season ambiguity.** Some beaches relax restrictions exactly on 30 September. Some relax them on 1 October. Some, when the autumn light is on the cliffs and you have the sand to yourself, leave the council sign up because no one has come to take it down. The legal date is the date on the sign. If it says 30 September, you are fine from 1 October. If the sign has come down already, you are fine immediately.

#### A last note, from inside the estate

We are five miles inland from Widemouth and seven from Bude, and we have 450 acres of our own. Dogs are welcome on every footpath on the estate. There is no seasonal restriction. There are no leads except past the alpaca paddock [internal link: /journal/a-morning-with-alberto-and-alfonzo] and past the bee field. There are dog-water bowls outside every main building and a deep-cleaning rinse-off station behind the W Club car park [internal link: /the-w-club].

If you are tired of negotiating beach signs, our own walks are the simplest answer. The 50-acre new woodland walk [internal link: /journal/the-fifty-acres-we-planted] is one of the best dog walks of the estate. The river path runs for almost two miles along the southern boundary. Both, in any weather, all year.

For a beach day, though, walk out of the cottage at 7.30am, drive to Sandymouth, be back for elevenses. That is the right shape of a Cornish dog morning.

### Article

**BLUF.** The Trelowen lodges at Whalesborough are built to a specification that exceeds BS3632 — the British Standard for residential park homes — by several measures, and are designed for a target useful life of 125 years. This is a craft-and-place piece about *how* we build them, the materials we chose, the trade-offs we made, and what two of the owners told us about living in one. It is not a piece about owning a lodge as a financial proposition.

[image: Wide image of the Trelowen development edge — three lodges visible at different stages of build, native woodland behind, the south-facing slope catching morning light.]

#### What BS3632 is, and what it isn't

The British Standard 3632, in its current form, is the residential specification for park homes and lodges in the United Kingdom. It sets out minimum requirements for insulation, structural integrity, fire safety, water and sewage connectivity, and a series of other factors that mean a home built to the standard is fit for year-round occupation.

It is, by design, a *minimum*. The expected lifespan of a BS3632-built lodge, on a maintained site, is around fifty years.

When we sat down with our build team in 2024 — we were five years into operating the estate, three years into the wider sustainability programme, and starting to feel the limit of what a lodge built to a standard residential-park spec could give a guest or, eventually, an owner — we asked a simple question.

What would it take to triple the spec?

#### Materials

The first decision was the frame.

The default Trelowen frame would have been a steel chassis with timber stud upper construction. This is the conventional, cost-efficient way to build a lodge — it goes up fast, it transports well, it meets the standard.

We instead specified a **British-grown oak post-and-beam frame**, paired with a thermally-broken steel chassis on the lower deck. The oak comes from a managed forest in Devon, certified through the Forest Stewardship Council, and the timber is cut, kilned and machined within the south-west of England. The build cost is materially higher. The lifespan, if maintained, is materially longer. Oak frame buildings of this kind — based on the records that we have access to from comparable projects — are at the 150-year end of the curve, not the 50-year end.

[image: Close-up of one of the oak posts in the frame of Lodge 4, the grain visible, the slight bevel of the joinery where the cross beam fits.]

The cladding is **Cornish larch**, harvested from a stand near Camelford. Larch is the right cladding species for this climate — it weathers to a soft silver, it is naturally durable without chemical treatment, and it sits visually in the landscape. We did consider Western Red Cedar, which has marginally better dimensional stability. We chose Cornish larch because it was Cornish.

The roof is a **British slate**, from Cumbria (Cornwall's own slate quarries have, with the exception of Delabole, largely closed in recent decades, and even Delabole's output is now mostly for restoration of listed buildings). We bought British slate because it lasts a century or more — synthetic alternatives commonly need replacement at 30 years.

The insulation is **wood fibre**, not foam. Wood fibre breathes, regulates humidity, and has a fraction of the embodied carbon of polyisocyanurate foam. It is more expensive per m². It is also significantly more thermally massive, which means the lodge keeps an even temperature through the day without aggressive heating.

The internal walls are **clay plaster** finished, not gypsum. Clay plaster wicks moisture, regulates humidity, and looks — visibly — like a hand-finished surface rather than a sprayed one. It is also one of the parts of the build that the lodge owners I have spoken to consistently mention.

#### The trade-offs

You do not get all of this for free. A few honest notes.

**Lead time.** A BS3632 standard lodge can be built and delivered in 12–16 weeks. A Trelowen build runs at 28–36 weeks. The oak frame alone takes 6 weeks to season and machine after felling. The clay plaster has a longer drying time. The site-finished joinery is slow.

**Cost.** We are not publishing the per-build cost in this article — that lives in the brochure [internal link: /stay/lodges/trelowen/brochure] for those who are considering ownership — but the build cost is substantially higher than a conventional lodge, and the price reflects that.

**Repairability vs. replaceability.** A BS3632 lodge is designed to be replaced. A Trelowen lodge is designed to be repaired. This matters in twenty years. It changes the cost of ownership profile in ways that are worth thinking about, but it requires a longer planning horizon. *[FCA REVIEW REQUIRED — any reference to cost-of-ownership profile must be reviewed for financial-promotion compliance before publication.]*

**Construction visibility.** During build, the Trelowen site is busier and dustier for longer. We have, in the past, had questions from guests in adjacent cottages. We now publish the build calendar [internal link: /stay/lodges/trelowen/build-schedule] so neighbouring stays can plan around it.

[pull-quote] *A BS3632 lodge is designed to be replaced. A Trelowen lodge is designed to be repaired. That matters in twenty years.*

#### The fitting-out

The kitchen is **British-made oak**, from a workshop in Frome. The worktops are a Cornish-quarried granite (from De Lank, near Bodmin) where the geology permits, or a fair-trade Indian Kashmir White for the lighter scheme. The taps are bronze, solid, made by a small foundry in the West Midlands.

The bathroom is *not* a manufactured pod. It is built in situ, with hand-laid Italian marble tile in the master and English-made ceramic in the second bath. The shower glass is laminated rather than tempered for longevity.

The flooring on the main level is engineered European oak, brushed and oiled, sourced from the same FSC-certified mill as the structural oak in the frame. The heating runs through the floor on a low-temperature wet system, fed by an air-source heat pump on the rear wall.

[image: Interior shot of the kitchen of Lodge 2, completed — oak cabinetry, granite worktop, the bronze tap catching light from the south-facing window.]

The lighting is on three independent circuits with dimmer control on each. The window joinery is locally-machined oak, triple-glazed, with the inner pane low-emissivity coated for thermal performance. The roof has a 35° pitch — steeper than the typical lodge roof — which is the angle the Cornish slate sits best at and which gives the building its silhouette.

The whole assembly is designed to last and to be looked after. There are no hidden voids in critical service runs. Every wiring loom is on a labelled circuit. Every plumbing run is accessible. The roof can be re-slated, the cladding can be replaced board-by-board, the floor can be re-oiled. *This is, perhaps, the single most important design principle on the project: nothing is finished forever, but everything can be maintained.*

#### What two owners told us

We asked two of the current Trelowen owners — both of whom we have spoken to with their permission, and whose details are anonymised — what they would say to someone considering one.

**Owner A** (early-60s, used the lodge primarily as a second home, 6–8 weeks of the year):

> "The thing nobody tells you about most park homes is that they are quiet in the wrong way — quiet because they're built thin and you're sitting inside the noise of the place. Trelowen is quiet differently. The walls have weight. You can hear the rain on the slate but it doesn't get inside the room. The first winter we stayed for ten days and didn't put the heating up past 19."

**Owner B** (mid-40s, used it as a family base for holidays with two teenagers, year-round):

> "We didn't know what wood-fibre insulation was when we signed. We do now because the place doesn't get clammy in August, which every rental cottage I've ever been in does. The kitchen — and this is a thing — has a smell of oak that you don't get anywhere else. The kids notice it. They walk in the door and the first thing they say is *the house smells like the house*."

We did not pre-script these answers. They are paraphrased from longer conversations and have been signed off by both owners.

The decision to buy a lodge is, for most people, a decision about a place rather than a number. We are happy to talk about the number — but it is not the part of the conversation that should happen first, and it is not the part of the conversation that this article is for.

What we can say, comfortably, is this. The Trelowen lodges are built to be lived in for a long time. They are built by Cornish trades, in Cornish materials, on a Cornish working farm. The wood under the plaster will, with reasonable care, outlast every one of us. That is — to us — the right reason to build.

### Article

**BLUF.** Whalesborough in October is the version of the estate that almost nobody photographs — quieter cottages, empty beaches, wood-burners lit by four in the afternoon, a low pewter sky over Bude Bay that turns gold for half an hour at sunset. A week in the season, told through what you do at each end of it.

[image: First-light October shot across the estate. Long shadow from the cottages, frost just lifting from the grass, two figures with a dog walking the lower field.]

October arrives at Whalesborough slowly.

It begins, properly, the second week of the month, when the morning frost first lays on the grass and the rooks regroup at the woodland edge. By then the August families have long gone, the September couples have rotated through, and what remains is a sort of held-breath quiet across the estate that almost no marketing photograph captures.

This is the bit of the year I have been trying to write about for three years and that I have, repeatedly, failed to do justice to.

#### Monday

You arrive on a Sunday afternoon — the cottages turn over on Sundays in autumn — and the light is already going. There is a wood-burner in every Trelowen and most of the cottages, and the welcome hamper on the kitchen island this month [internal link: /journal/whats-in-your-welcome-hamper-this-month] contains a small bag of seasoned ash from the estate, with a card explaining how to light it without smoking the room out.

You light it.

[image: A cast-iron wood-burner door open, kindling neatly built, a single match held to it. The hand in the shot wears no jewellery — a Cornish working hand.]

By Monday morning the place has started to settle around you. You have a coffee from the kitchen in a heavy cup. You stand at the south-facing window of the cottage and you look down the slope towards the river and there is — and this is the thing — *no one in the field.* The summer holiday parc of August is gone. The estate has reverted to being what it actually is: a working farm at the end of the harvest, edged with cottages, with a slow river running through it.

You walk down to *The Weir* [internal link: /the-weir-restaurant] for the lunchtime menu. The kitchen is doing the slow autumn things. The Neetfield squash has come in. There is a roast hake from a Bude boat with brown butter and capers. The bread is the same Baker Tom's stoneground as the hamper. You sit at a window table and there are perhaps eight other diners. The conversation in the room is at speaking volume rather than at restaurant pitch.

#### Tuesday

It rains on Tuesday. It will rain at least once during your week — this is north Cornwall in October, and the wet Atlantic systems come through about every three days.

[pull-quote] *Cornwall in the wet, in autumn, is one of the more underrated atmospheric pleasures in the British Isles.*

This is not a disaster. Cornwall in the wet, in autumn, is one of the more underrated atmospheric pleasures in the British Isles. The cliffs at Crackington [internal link: /journal/dog-friendly-cornwall-honestly] in driven rain are something close to a Caspar David Friedrich painting. The Bude breakwater in a heavy October squall, the spray going clean over the lighthouse, is a thing worth seeing once.

But if you do not want to do any of that, you do the indoor version of Tuesday. You go down to the W Club [internal link: /the-w-club] in the morning during the adult-only hours [internal link: /journal/why-we-built-the-w-club-around-quiet] and you have the pool essentially to yourself. You book the *Coast Walk* deep-tissue ninety in the afternoon. The rosemary is still cut on the morning of, even in October — Anya, who runs the botanical garden, says the rosemary is at its most aromatic in the early autumn, just before the first hard frost.

You eat in the cottage in the evening. You cook something slow with what you bought at Robins Farm Shop in Stratton on the way down. The wood-burner is well established by now. There is no light pollution. If the sky has cleared by midnight, you go and stand on the deck and look up.

#### Wednesday

The midweek day is when the estate is at its most itself.

There is a small thing that happens in the dawn light around 7am in October on the upper field. The deer come down from the woodland edge to feed at the perimeter of the new planting [internal link: /journal/the-fifty-acres-we-planted]. Usually a roe doe and one or two yearlings. They are visible from the upper Trelowen lodges if you happen to be up early enough.

[image: Roe deer at the edge of Block 4 of the woodland planting, mist in the foreground, the planting tubes barely visible behind. Early.]

You go and watch the alpacas [internal link: /journal/a-morning-with-alberto-and-alfonzo]. You walk the woodland ride. You come back at the right side of midday for a slow lunch and a book by the fire.

The autumn produce in The Weir's kitchen is at peak this week. The squash. The cobnuts from a Cornish hedgerow grower near St Tudy. The first of the apples from a small orchard outside Boscastle. The chef has been working on a cobnut and rosemary brown butter — it appears on the dinner menu midweek and is, in my view, the single best thing on the autumn list this year.

#### Thursday

You walk the long version of Sandymouth on Thursday. The car park is half-empty. The lifeguards are gone for the season. The dog [internal link: /journal/dog-friendly-cornwall-honestly] is off-lead the whole way. The wind off the Atlantic is sharp now — proper jacket weather — but the sand at low tide stretches for a kilometre at the south end and you can walk in a straight line for thirty minutes without turning.

You see two other people. You see eleven gulls. You see one cormorant on a black rock further out.

[image: Wide low-angle of Sandymouth, the cliffs running north into the autumn haze, a single small figure with a dog in the mid-distance.]

You come back via Bude town, which has the autumn rhythm: half the tourist shops have shut for the season, the surf shop is having a 30%-off sale on last season's wetsuits, the chip shop on the canal is busier than the cafés. You buy fish and chips. You eat them on the bench by the lifeboat station, watching the wind off the bay.

#### Friday

The last full day of the week, you start to think about leaving, and the leaving thought is — and this is the thing — sharper in autumn than in any other season. The place becomes more itself the longer you stay in it. By Friday you have learned the noises the cottage makes. You have a preferred chair. The wood-burner is no longer something you light, it is something you maintain.

[pull-quote] *The place becomes more itself the longer you stay in it. By Friday you have learned the noises the cottage makes.*

You walk one last time. You eat one last lunch at The Weir, this time at the bar. You drink a glass of the Cornish dry cider from a small producer near Truro that the bar list carries in autumn. You buy a jar of honey from reception to take home — the September 2025 harvest, of which there are now only a few jars left until the new harvest in autumn 2026.

#### Saturday — going home

The cottage emptied of you. The hamper basket goes back. The wood-burner ash, cool, goes into the small metal pail with the lid. You leave at eleven. The cottages turn over on a Sunday again now, in autumn — Friday-to-Monday short breaks are the most common pattern from October through to mid-December.

The drive home is in the going-back direction along the A39, and the season is going past at the window: hawthorn berries red in the hedge, the cob nuts in the dropping leaves, the low sun coming in across the inland fields at a tilt that you don't get in any other month.

You are already thinking about coming back at Christmas.

This is the trick of an October week at Whalesborough. The cottages are quieter and the rate is lower and the light is shorter and somehow, in spite of all of that, the place gives you more.

If you want to read more about the season, the rest of the **journal** [internal link: /journal] runs through November planting, December's first hard frost, and the long winter mornings on the estate. The autumn short-break rate [internal link: /stay/short-breaks/autumn] runs from October through to the third week of December.

You should also know that this article is part of the **seasonal mood selector** on the homepage [internal link: /]. The estate has four versions of itself across the year, and we have tried to write each of them honestly. October is, between you and me, my favourite.
