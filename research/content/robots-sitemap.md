# Robots, Sitemap & AI Crawler Specification

**Project:** Whalesborough Farm Resort & Spa
**Domain:** whalesborough.co.uk
**Status:** Production Specification — 2026-05-14
**Owner:** Technical SEO Lead (Peake Management)
**Stack:** Next.js 15 (App Router) on Cloudflare Workers, Sanity CMS, deployed via GitHub Actions
**Total user-facing routes:** 107 canonical URLs across eight functional silos

---

## 1. Strategic Context

In May 2026 the search visibility battle is being fought on three fronts simultaneously: classical Google ranking (still ~58 percent of resort discovery clicks), AI Overviews and generative answer surfaces (Google AI Overviews, Bing Copilot, Perplexity, ChatGPT Search), and emerging LLM-native retrieval where ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini cite sources directly from training-time crawls. Whalesborough must be machine-readable on all three.

The robots/sitemap layer is the most over-looked of the three. A misconfigured `robots.txt` will silently exclude us from AI Overviews. A poorly segmented sitemap will leave 30 percent of inner cottage pages un-indexed for months. Aggressive scrapers (MJ12bot, DotBot, eight-figure-a-month SaaS surveillance crawlers) can consume Cloudflare budget that should be going to real users.

This document defines the full specification for production day one, plus the operational runbook for the six months after launch when Google Search Console coverage reports tell us what is actually being indexed.

### Why we segment seven sitemaps not one

Google's hard limit is 50,000 URLs or 50MB uncompressed per sitemap. We are not close to that — the full site is ~400 URLs at launch and probably ~700 within twelve months as the journal grows. But segmenting by content type gives us three operational wins:

1. **Faster diagnosis.** When Search Console says "twelve URLs not indexed," knowing they are all in `sitemap-stay.xml` collapses the debugging time from days to minutes.
2. **Differential refresh cadence.** Cottages re-publish weekly via Sanity webhooks. The journal pushes new articles three times a week. Legal pages change yearly. Building one giant sitemap forces all of them onto the slowest cadence; segmentation lets each silo declare its own `<lastmod>` strategy.
3. **Bing and Yandex compliance.** Both engines parse sitemap-index files faster than monolithic sitemaps, and Yandex still chokes on files above 10MB.

### Notes on the `robots.txt` design

- **Order matters.** Per RFC 9309 (the September 2022 standardisation of `robots.txt`) crawlers select the most specific user-agent block. We list `*` first as a defensive default, then override per crawler.
- **Disallow rules are not access control.** Anyone can ignore `robots.txt`. The `/admin/`, `/owners/`, `/account/` paths are also protected by Cloudflare Access (Zero Trust SSO) and Next.js middleware that returns 401 for unauthenticated requests. The `Disallow:` directive is courtesy only — never security.
- **Lodge-sales financial-promotion pages.** Three URLs (`/own/rental-income`, `/own/objection-handling`, `/own/the-costs`) carry FCA-regulated investment promotion content. We allow Google indexing because UK case law treats search snippets as journalistic, but block AI synthesis until our compliance lawyer signs off on the AI policy in Q3 2026.
- **Query-string disallows.** Tracking parameters (`utm_*`, `gclid`, `fbclid`) and session tokens are blocked from indexing to prevent duplicate-content dilution. Real users still see them; crawlers do not waste budget on infinite-variant URLs.
- **No `Allow:` after broad `Disallow:`.** A common mistake is `Disallow: /` followed by `Allow: /public/`. Major crawlers honour this but Yandex does not. We default to allow-all and only Disallow specific paths.
- **Cloudflare's AI Audit overlay.** Even with `Allow:`, every AI crawler request is logged to Cloudflare Logpush and Sentry for legal audit. If OpenAI or Anthropic violates the disallow rules, we have evidence in 30 days of logs.

## 5. Per-URL Sitemap Entry Format

Every `<url>` element follows this canonical pattern. The example below is the Arvor Suites detail page.

```xml
<url>
  <loc>https://whalesborough.co.uk/stay/arvor-suites/clifftop</loc>
  <lastmod>2026-05-12T14:23:00+01:00</lastmod>
  <changefreq>weekly</changefreq>
  <priority>0.8</priority>
  <xhtml:link rel="alternate" hreflang="en-GB" href="https://whalesborough.co.uk/stay/arvor-suites/clifftop"/>
  <xhtml:link rel="alternate" hreflang="x-default" href="https://whalesborough.co.uk/stay/arvor-suites/clifftop"/>
  <image:image>
    <image:loc>https://cdn.whalesborough.co.uk/arvor/clifftop-hero.avif</image:loc>
    <image:title>Arvor Clifftop Suite</image:title>
    <image:caption>Floor-to-ceiling Atlantic views from the clifftop Arvor suite.</image:caption>
  </image:image>
</url>
```

### Rules

- **`<loc>`:** Absolute URL, canonical form, no trailing slash, lowercase, no `.html` suffix, no query string, no fragment.
- **`<lastmod>`:** ISO 8601 with timezone. Refreshed at build time via `next-sitemap` plugin, then overridden by Sanity webhooks when content changes.
- **`<changefreq>`:** Hint, not directive. Google increasingly ignores it but Bing and Yandex still respect it. We populate it accurately to keep all engines honest.
- **`<priority>`:** Relative weight inside our own sitemap. Has no effect on cross-site ranking but helps engines budget their crawl when resources are constrained. We use a 1.0 / 0.9 / 0.8 / 0.7 / 0.6 / 0.5 / 0.4 / 0.3 scale and reserve 1.0 for the homepage only.
- **`<image:image>`:** Required namespace `xmlns:image="http://www.google.com/schemas/sitemap-image/1.1"` declared on the root `<urlset>` element. Hero image only in the per-URL entry; gallery images live in `sitemap-images.xml`.
- **`<news:news>`:** Required for any journal post ≤48 hours old. Namespace `xmlns:news="http://www.google.com/schemas/sitemap-news/0.9"`. Includes publication name, language, publication date and title.

## 6. Sitemap Generation Strategy

### Build-time generation (Next.js)

Each segment is generated by an `app/sitemap-*.ts` route handler returning an array of URL entries. Implementation files:

- `app/sitemap.ts` — sitemap index
- `app/sitemap-static.ts`
- `app/sitemap-stay.ts`
- `app/sitemap-spa.ts`
- `app/sitemap-dine.ts`
- `app/sitemap-own.ts`
- `app/sitemap-estate.ts`
- `app/sitemap-journal.ts`
- `app/sitemap-images.ts`
- `app/sitemap-legal.ts`

Each handler queries Sanity for the current set of slugs, formats them per the spec, and returns. Next.js caches the response at the Cloudflare edge with a 1-hour TTL.

### On-demand revalidation

Sanity webhooks fire on every content publish, calling `POST /api/revalidate-sitemap` with the affected document type. The endpoint maps document types to sitemap segments and calls `revalidateTag()` for that segment only — never the whole sitemap index. A typical revalidation completes in <300ms and the new XML is live at the edge within 60 seconds.

### Cron-based regeneration

A Cloudflare Cron Trigger runs at 03:00 UTC daily, calling `POST /api/regenerate-all-sitemaps`. This is the belt-and-braces backup if a Sanity webhook is missed (network blip, retry exhaustion). It also updates the `<lastmod>` on the sitemap-index entries to today, signalling Google to re-crawl even when no content has changed.

### IndexNow integration

On every successful sitemap regeneration we POST the affected URLs to IndexNow's shared endpoint at `https://api.indexnow.org/IndexNow`. This pings Bing, Yandex, Seznam, Naver and (since November 2024) Microsoft Copilot in Edge. Google does not yet accept IndexNow but has signalled they may. Each ping carries our IndexNow key file at `/d8b9e2a0-whalesborough.txt` for verification.

## 7. AI Crawler Considerations

### Audit-grade logging

Every request matching a known AI-crawler user-agent is logged via Cloudflare Logpush to Sentry with a custom tag `crawler:ai`. Logs retain 30 days hot, 365 days cold storage. This gives us legal evidence if any LLM provider scrapes content we have disallowed.

Fields captured per request: timestamp, IP (anonymised to /24), user-agent, path, status code, response bytes, referer, ASN, country. ASN allows us to spot when a crawler claims to be ClaudeBot but originates from an unverified AS — a common indicator of UA spoofing.

## 9. Canonical URL Strategy

Every page declares a self-referential canonical pointing to its primary URL form. This is critical because filter views, pagination and tracking parameters can otherwise create thousands of duplicate URLs.

### Rules

- **Self-canonical default.** `<link rel="canonical" href="https://whalesborough.co.uk/[path]">` on every page.
- **Filter view canonicals.** `/stay/cottages?sleeps=6&dogs=yes` canonicals back to `/stay/cottages`. The filtered view is indexable behind the parent but not preferred for ranking.
- **Pagination.** `/journal?page=2` uses `<link rel="next">` / `<link rel="prev">` plus a self-canonical to itself (not to page 1). Google deprecated rel=prev/next in 2019 but Bing and Yandex still use it, and major LLM crawlers honour the signal.
- **Trailing slash policy.** No trailing slash on canonical URLs. A 301 redirect at the Cloudflare Worker edge strips trailing slashes server-side. Exceptions: `/` for root only.
- **Lowercase.** All URLs lowercase; 301 redirects from any uppercase variant.
- **No `www` subdomain.** `www.whalesborough.co.uk` 301 redirects to apex `whalesborough.co.uk`.
- **HTTPS only.** HTTP requests 301 redirect to HTTPS; HSTS preload enforced.
- **No query strings in canonical.** Tracking parameters (utm_*, gclid, fbclid, ref) are stripped from canonical declarations regardless of what is in the URL bar.
