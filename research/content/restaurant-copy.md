## /dine — Hub Page

### Hero
**H1:** The Weir.
**Subhead:** Breakfast, lunch and dinner on the lake.

### Editorial Intro (200 words)

The Weir sits on the edge of the fishing lake, glazed wall to wall on the south side, so the room moves with the light and the swans. The kitchen runs a paddock-to-plate menu — not as a slogan, as a route. Ninety percent of what reaches the pass comes from inside a five-mile radius. The sausages walk down from Snout & Trotter in Marhamchurch. The lamb grazes the field you can see from the terrace. The cheese is made in the dairy a hundred yards from the back door.

Mornings are slower. Free-range eggs, smoked bacon, sourdough toast, a pot of tea, the lake. From noon the day menu opens — soup from whatever Neetfield Market Garden has pulled that week, a Phillip Warren steak burger that the regulars argue is the best in Bude, Whalesborough cheese boards built for sharing. Summer Friday and Saturday evenings, the parilla grill is fired up and dinner becomes an event.

Dogs are welcome inside and out. Children have their own menu and a play area in eyeshot of the terrace. There's no dress code. There's a log fire in winter and parasols in July.

Reserve a table, or walk in. Both work.

## /dine/paddock-to-plate — Sourcing Story

### Hero
**H1:** Paddock to plate. Counted in fields, not air miles.

### Body Copy (300 words)

Most restaurants in Cornwall say farm-to-table. We use paddock-to-plate because it's literal. The lamb you'll see on Friday's grill grazed on Whalesborough land last week. The cheese on the antipasti board was turned by hand three buildings away. The mushrooms in the morning crumpet came across the bridge from Neetfield Market Garden, picked the same morning the chef started service.

The five-mile radius is the rule we set ourselves. Anything that can be grown, reared, fished, smoked, baked or roasted inside that circle, we buy from the people inside that circle. Hoof to Block, our butcher, is ten minutes' drive inland. They run nose-to-tail, so the cuts on the lunch menu rotate with what's been broken down that week — and the trimmings end up in the sausages we serve at breakfast. Snout & Trotter sits a mile up the road in Marhamchurch; Chris Jopling makes pork sausages the way his grandfather made pork sausages, from happy free-range Cornish pigs.

What we can't grow or rear ourselves, we buy from people we know by first name. Celtic Fish & Game, based in St Ives since 1985, drive the day-boat catch up the A30. Tregida Smokehouse smokes the mackerel on the salad. Cornish Coffee Company hand-roasts the beans for the morning Americano. Trewithen Dairy in Lostwithiel sends the milk, the yoghurt and the clotted cream. Olivers North Coast Wines in Bude curates the list, and they'll tell you the grower's name for every bottle.

The result is a menu that changes with what's ready, what's landed and what's been butchered. If the lamb sausage isn't on the board, it's because the lamb hasn't been cut yet. That's the deal.

## /dine/suppliers — Supplier Directory

### Hero
**H1:** Who feeds The Weir.
**Subhead:** Eleven producers. None more than thirty miles away.

### Intro Copy (60 words)

Every dish on the menu carries a supplier tag. Tap a dish in the app and you'll see who grew, reared or caught the ingredients. Below, the people behind the food, in their own words where possible, in ours where we've translated.

### Supplier Bios

**Whalesborough Cheese — on the farm**
Started seventeen years ago in the dairy three buildings from the restaurant. Small-batch, hand-turned, made from pasteurised cow's and goat's milk from the herd next door. The three cheeses you'll see most often on our boards are Smuggler (firm, hard, nutty), Miss Muffet (washed-rind, semi-soft, faintly farmyardy in the best way) and Nanny Muffet (goats' milk, mild, clean). Rick Stein stocks them in Padstow. The cheesemaker walks past the restaurant on the way to work.

**Hoof to Block — Bradworthy, 10 miles inland**
Our butcher. Grass-fed, high-welfare meat from farms across north Devon and Cornwall, broken down nose-to-tail. The Phillip Warren steak in the burger comes through them; so do the cuts that turn up as Wednesday's specials. They cut to order, twice a week, and the offcuts feed the sausage line at Snout & Trotter.

**Neetfield Market Garden — on the farm**
Across the bridge at the start of the drive, on the left. Vegetables, salads, soft fruit, herbs. Run on regenerative principles — no synthetic fertiliser, cover-cropped, hand-weeded. The soup of the day is whatever Neetfield has too much of that morning. If you ask the chef what the salad came from, they'll point at the field.

**Land Meets Sea — 1 mile from the coast**
Farmer Dan's flock of permanent-pasture lamb and beef, grazed on meadow grass within a mile of the cliffs at Widemouth Bay. One-hundred-percent outdoor, low-stocking-density, slow-matured. Sustainability isn't a marketing line here — it's the only way the system works. You'll often see Dan's sheep in the paddocks visible from the restaurant terrace.

**Snout & Trotter — Marhamchurch, 1 mile**
Chris Jopling's free-range Cornish pork sausages, made by traditional method on the edge of the village. The Marhamchurch sausage on the breakfast plate is his. So is the dog treat.

**Celtic Fish & Game — St Ives, established 1985**
Day-boat fish, in-season game. Family-run, three generations deep. The hake, the salmon, the catch-of-the-day all come up the A30 on ice and reach the kitchen before lunch.

**Tregida Smokehouse — Padstow**
Mackerel smoked over Cornish oak. The fillets on the smoked mackerel jacket and the salad add-on come from Tregida.

**Phillip Warren Butchers — Launceston**
Beef. The 6oz Warren burger is named after them. Dry-aged, grass-fed, traceable to the farm.

**Cornish Coffee Company — Cornwall**
Hand-roasted single-origin coffee, sourced direct from individual farms. Every cup of Americano, latte and flat white poured at The Weir is Cornish Coffee.

**Trewithen Dairy — Lostwithiel**
Milk, yoghurt, clotted cream and butter. Family-run, herd of British Friesians, milk processed within hours of leaving the cow. The clotted cream on the cream tea is theirs.

**Olivers North Coast Wines — Bude**
Local merchant, twenty minutes' drive. Curates a wine list that balances Cornish producers with small growers from France, Italy and Spain. They write the tasting notes on the back of the wine list themselves.

**John Patt's Ltd — Cornwall**
The wholesaler for everything Cornwall produces that we don't grow ourselves: salad bags, root veg in winter, citrus. Local-first, always.

**Whalesborough Honey — on the farm**
Hives on the estate. Honey in the granola, honey on the hot cross bun, honey on the cheeseboard.

## /dine/events — Events Hub

### Hero
**H1:** What's on at The Weir.
**Subhead:** Six things a year you should plan a trip around.

### Editorial Intro (150 words)

The Weir runs six recurring event types across the year, plus a handful of one-offs the chef pencils in when something turns up — a glut of asparagus, a guest pitmaster, a Wednesday in February when nothing else is happening. Tickets and tables for everything below sit inside the app from this season onward. Lakeside Locals see the line-up seven days before public release and get the front-row tables on the supper clubs.

Anything that can be walked to is welcome on foot. Anything that wants the parilla grill fired up needs at least a fortnight's notice for the supplier orders. Anything family-sized, dog-included, kids-running-loose-by-the-lake is a Sunday.

Below, the six fixtures, what to expect, and what they cost.

### Event 1 — Grill and Chill (100 words)

**Fortnightly Fridays from May to September. From 17:00 until late.**

The parilla grill comes outside, the sides come down on the terrace, and the kitchen cooks over wood and coal until the sun drops behind the lodge field. Expect Hoof to Block lamb chops, Whalesborough lamb sausage, charred summer vegetables from Neetfield, the chimichurri chicken skewer, and whatever the chef's curing or fermenting that week. Eat in courses or graze. Bar open with Cornish ciders, Korev on draught, and a one-page natural-wine list. Family-friendly, dog-friendly, walk-in welcome but a table will get you the lakeside spot. Tickets from £18 per head.

### Event 2 — Supper Club (100 words)

**One Thursday a month. 19:00 to 22:30. Twenty-four covers, communal seating.**

A five-course set menu built around a single supplier — January with Hoof to Block on nose-to-tail, March with Celtic Fish & Game on whatever the boats landed that week, June with Neetfield on the season's vegetables. The producer eats with us; you sit alongside them, ask whatever you want, and learn what was hanging upside-down for fourteen days before you ate it. Wine pairings curated by Olivers North Coast Wines, six glasses, six growers. £75 per head with paired wines; £55 without. Twelve places held for Locals; twelve released to the public.

### Event 3 — Galentine's: Sip, Spa, Paint (100 words)

**Daily, 10th to 28th February. 11:00 to 15:00.**

Crossover with the spa at The W Club. Three hours, four parts: a 30-minute treatment of your choice, full use of the spa pool, sauna and steam room, a bottle of Prosecco (or alcohol-free Nosecco) on the terrace at The Weir, and a guided painting workshop with all materials provided. Run with a group of friends or join a mixed table — usually six women, two bottles of fizz, one canvas each, a lot of laughing. £95 per person, all-in. Booking through the app; spa side handled in the background by the W Club team.

### Event 4 — Easter at The Weir (100 words)

**Good Friday to Easter Monday. Lunch service 12:00 to 16:00; Easter Sunday extended to 17:00.**

Hot cross buns from the bakery through breakfast, with Whalesborough honey or homemade jam on the side. Easter Sunday is the big one — the regular Sunday roast joined by two specials: lamb chops from Land Meets Sea (the flock on the field next door), and a homemade shepherd's pie made from the same lamb's shoulder. Kids' chocolate hunt around the wildlife trail starts at 11:00, free with a roast booking. Easter Sunday tables are booking-only and go fast — open the app eight weeks before the date.

### Event 5 — Music with Yazzy (100 words)

**Selected Saturdays through summer, plus the Sunday of Valentine's weekend. 14:00 to 16:00.**

Local jazz and soul singer Yazzy plays acoustic on the terrace, no amplification, the lake doing the rest of the work. Two-hour slots, drinks bar open, kitchen running an abbreviated lunch menu — cream teas, sharing boards, a soup, a salad. No cover charge. The trick is to book a terrace table from noon, eat lunch slowly and let the music start around you. Walk-ins welcome at the bar but the seated terrace tables fill out by mid-morning every time. Locals get the lakeside row.

### Event 6 — Sunday Roast (100 words)

**Every Sunday, 12:00 to 17:00. Last roast served at 16:00.**

The most-asked-for thing on the menu, every week. A choice of three meats: Land Meets Sea beef rib, Hoof to Block lamb shoulder, free-range Snout & Trotter pork loin. Duck-fat roast potatoes, seasonal vegetables from Neetfield, cauliflower cheese, Yorkshire pudding, red-wine gravy. £19.50 per adult, £8.50 per child. Vegetarian and vegan roasts on a separate sheet — properly cooked, not an afterthought. Walk-in fine for two; tables of four or more, book in the app. Locals see availability open from the Monday before.
