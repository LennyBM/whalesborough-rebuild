## /estate — Estate Hub

### BLUF
Whalesborough is a 500-acre working estate on the north Cornwall coast, two and a half miles inland from Bude and a fifteen-minute walk from the South West Coast Path. Farmland, woodland, lake, meadow, market garden and a sea-air microclimate sit inside one continuous boundary — managed for guests, residents, and roughly six hundred farmed and wild creatures.

### Hero pitch — 400 words

Walk past the spa, past the farm gate, past the new oak plantation, and after about twelve minutes the sound of the dual carriageway disappears entirely. What you hear after that is the estate: a tractor on a low gear two fields away, sheep being moved between the lower paddock and the lake meadow, jackdaws over the woodland, and — if the wind is from the west — the long, low note of the Atlantic breaking on the Hartland cliffs ten miles north.

This is the part of Whalesborough no map can communicate. The 500 acres are not a backdrop. They are a working operation. A flock of grazing sheep keeps the meadow short for the wildflowers. Fifteen thousand Cornish Black Bees pollinate the market garden that supplies the Weir kitchen. Two alpacas — Alberto and Alfonzo — have a route they take through the lower fields every morning, and they will let you walk with them on Wednesdays and Sundays at two. Tim, the estate manager, has been here twenty-three years. He knows where the kingfisher nests and which gate the badgers prefer.

We grew from 450 acres to just over 500 in 2024 with the addition of the Neetfield parcel, and the way we describe ourselves now is honest: not a country park, not a petting farm, not a glamping site dressed up in flannel. A working Cornish estate that opens most of its land to guests, that earns its rest tea from twenty hours of fence-mending, and that produces — slowly, properly — the food on your plate at dinner.

The estate is organised, on the ground and in this app, around four entry points. Start where you like.

**Map** — The interactive map. Where you are, where everything is, which fields are active right now, where the dog bowls are, where the sun sits at five.

**Walks** — Eight signed walks from twenty-minute toddler-loops to a six-hour day on the Hartland coast. Each with grade, gradient, surface, gate count, dog notes, and a real photograph from this season.

**Activities** — Thirty-plus bookable things. Some free, some £25, some £450. Beekeeping with Tim at the apiary, sunrise yoga in the meadow, an early-hour sole-use of the spa pool. All schedulable, none compulsory.

**Farm** — The working part. Animals, market garden, beehives, the people who run them. Honest about what we do and what we don't.

## /estate/walks — Walks Hub

### BLUF
Eight signed walks lead off the estate, ranging from the easy 1.5-mile Whalesborough Loop suitable for buggies and older dogs, to the six-hour, 11-mile day out on the Hartland Heritage Coast. All are mapped offline in this app with what3words for trickier turns, gradient profiles, a recent ground-condition photo updated weekly, and honest difficulty grades. Most allow dogs.

### Editorial intro — 300 words

There is a particular pleasure in walking from a hot bath, through the orchard, past the apiary, out across the lake meadow, and onto the South West Coast Path without ever crossing a tarmac road. Whalesborough is one of the few estates in Cornwall where that is possible — the canal towpath connects directly into the SWCP via a footbridge half a mile from the farm gate, and from there the entire 630-mile route is yours.

We have signed eight walks from the estate, each chosen for a particular hour and a particular kind of weather. The Whalesborough Loop is what you do on the first morning when you've not yet unstiffened from the drive. The SWCP Phillips Point walk is what you do when the forecast clears at three and you want a wide sky for the last hour of light. The Crackington Haven walk is for the day you want to feel earned. The Bude Canal towpath is for the day you want flat ground and an ice-cream at the end.

Each walk has its own page — grade (A/B/C using the National Trust grading system), distance, ascent, gate count, surface notes (stiles, kissing gates, mud-after-rain), dog suitability with specific notes (sheep field at point 4, off-lead OK from point 7), parking and public transit notes for the start and end points, refreshments en route, and a recent ground-condition photo. We update those photos weekly through the season.

All walks except one (the Crackington coastal) are dog-friendly under proper recall. Dogs on lead through any field with stock, please. Sheep do not negotiate.

### The eight walks

#### 1. Whalesborough Loop — 1.5 miles, easy (Grade A)

**Whalesborough estate to Whalesborough estate. Buggies, older dogs, and post-Sunday-roast.**

The orientation walk. Out from the farm gate, anticlockwise round the lake meadow, up the gentle rise to the orchard bench, back through the new oak plantation. Forty minutes at a wandering pace, twenty-five if you're moving. No road crossings, four wooden gates, all kissing-gate width. The lake meadow is signed off-lead for dogs with good recall — there are no sheep in this paddock between April and October, only the resident geese, who will see you off if you get within ten feet.

The orchard bench at point three sits under a forty-year-old Cornish Aromatic apple tree. Sit. The view runs across the lake to the Neetfield woods, with the Atlantic horizon line just visible above the canopy on a clear day. In May the meadow is yellow rattle and orchid; in August it's knee-high grass and dragonflies; in October it's mist until eleven.

Surface: mown grass throughout, slightly soft for two weeks after heavy rain. Buggy-friendly with one short pushed section by the lake. Dogs on lead in the orchard (apple-windfall risk in autumn).

Start what3words: ///quoting.spotted.thatched. Suitable for all ages.

#### 2. Canal-to-Bude — 3 miles each way, easy (Grade A), dog-friendly

**Whalesborough farm gate to Bude harbour via the canal towpath. Flat. Buggy-friendly. Beer-and-chips at the end.**

The most-walked route from the estate. Cross the lower paddock, through the canal-side gate (off-lead area immediately on your left if your dog needs a run-around), and onto the Bude Canal towpath. Three miles in flat, with the canal on your left and the wet meadow on your right. Curlew, little egret, the occasional kingfisher at the third lock. The path is hard-packed gravel — buggies, wheelchairs and trikes all viable.

The towpath delivers you straight into Bude at the sea-lock — the only canal sea-lock still operating in the UK. From there it's three minutes to Summerleaze Beach, five minutes to the Sea Pool, eight minutes to The Brendon Arms (dog-friendly, real ales, food until nine). Return the same way, or jump a taxi for £8 if legs are done.

Dogs welcome throughout. Lead on the towpath proper (cyclists), off-lead in the off-lead meadow. Water bowls at the third lock and at the harbour pubs.

Start what3words: ///quoting.spotted.thatched. End what3words: ///parts.thus.parade. Suitable for all ages and most mobilities.

#### 3. SWCP Phillips Point — 4 miles, moderate (Grade B), dramatic

**Estate to Phillips Point and back via the South West Coast Path. Four hours with photos. Big sky.**

The walk we send people on for the cinematic hour before dinner. Out via the canal-to-Bude route (above), but at the harbour bear north on the SWCP. The path climbs out of Bude past Compass Point — a small octagonal stone tower used by storm-watchers — and onto the cliff path proper. From there, two miles of open coast to Phillips Point, with the sea three hundred feet below on your left and grazed pasture to your right. On a south-westerly, the spray reaches you. On a still evening in June, you can hear seals on Northcott Mouth.

Phillips Point itself is a small, signed promontory with a stone bench. Slate-blue water broken white at the bar mouth — the long, sustained roll of the swell into Northcott Mouth — and on a clear day, Lundy Island as a low grey shape on the northern horizon.

Surface: tarmac and packed gravel through Bude, then mown cliff path with two short, steep sections (rope handrail provided). Stiles: four. Gradient: 90m total ascent. Not buggy-suitable. Dogs on lead throughout (sheep on cliff pasture, cliff edge unfenced).

Start what3words: ///quoting.spotted.thatched. Best between two hours before sunset and an hour after. Allow four hours including stops.

#### 4. Marhamchurch Village — 2 miles loop, easy (Grade A), historic

**Estate to Marhamchurch village and back via the church footpath. Two hours. Pub lunch optional.**

The local village walk. Out from the farm gate via the eastern field, over a single stile, onto the old church path that has been walked since the eleventh century. Marhamchurch is a small, tight, stone village with a Norman church (St Marwenne's, named for the seventh-century Cornish saint who is said to have founded a hermitage on this site), a community shop selling Cornish cheese and local bread, and one pub — The Bullers Arms, which welcomes dogs and walking boots and serves a perfectly proper ploughman's.

The return route loops south through Sutton Wood, a small mixed broadleaf wood managed by the Woodland Trust. Bluebells in early May, fungi in October. The path can hold water through January and February — wellies advised.

Surface: mostly grass and packed earth, with two short tarmac sections through the village. Three stiles (medium height — older dogs may need a lift). Buggy-suitable through the village, less so through the wood.

Start what3words: ///quoting.spotted.thatched. Pub kitchen closes 2pm and 9pm. Suitable for most ages and mobilities except buggies in winter wood.

#### 5. Hartland Heritage Coast preview — 5 miles each way, moderate (Grade B+)

**A taste of the wildest coast in southern Britain. Five miles, four hours, lifetime view.**

The Hartland Heritage Coast — sixty miles of cliffs running from Bude north into north Devon — is the most dramatic stretch of the entire SWCP. We are five miles from where it properly begins. This walk takes you on the southernmost five miles, from Sandy Mouth car park (5-minute drive or 30-minute taxi from the estate) north to Duckpool, where you turn and return.

The cliffs here are the highest in Cornwall — Higher Sharpnose Point rises 466 feet straight out of the sea, and the path traces the very edge. Below, the rock strata are folded into sharp vertical creases — a geological feature known locally as "the ribs," visible at low tide. Seals on every visit. Peregrine on most. Storm-petrels in October. In autumn, the bracken on the slopes turns a deep, almost crimson rust.

This is a Grade B+ walk: not technical, but exposed, with no shelter and no phone signal for stretches. Carry water, layers, a real waterproof. Walking poles useful. Five sets of steep wooden steps; total ascent 180m.

Surface: cliff path, mostly grass and rock. Five stiles. Dogs on lead throughout — cliff edge is unfenced. Not buggy-suitable. Not wheelchair-suitable. Drive to Sandy Mouth start: ///mismatch.parks.amusement. Park at NT car park (free for members, £6 otherwise).

#### 6. Bude Canal towpath — distances vary (1 to 12 miles each way), easy (Grade A)

**The full length of the canal, as far as your legs want to go. Flat. Pub-spotted.**

The Bude Canal originally ran for thirty-five miles from Bude inland to Launceston, built in the 1820s to bring sea-sand inland as agricultural lime. Most of it is now derelict, but the first two miles from the sea-lock are perfectly maintained, dog-friendly, and run directly past our farm gate.

Walk as far as you like. The towpath is hard-packed gravel throughout — buggy, wheelchair and gravel-bike friendly. Notable stopping points: Helebridge (3 miles in, café, swans, picnic benches), Whalesborough farm gate (4 miles in — i.e. straight back to your cottage if you need it), Marhamchurch Inclined Plane (5 miles in — the Victorian wagon-lift, now a heritage exhibit), and Pethericks Mill (7 miles in, woodland picnic spot).

Dogs welcome throughout, lead recommended on the towpath itself (cyclists), off-lead in the dog meadow at the Whalesborough gate. Water bowls at Helebridge café, Whalesborough farm gate, Marhamchurch Inn, and Pethericks Mill.

Start what3words: ///parts.thus.parade (Bude sea-lock) or ///quoting.spotted.thatched (Whalesborough farm gate). Best in any weather except a strong west wind, which funnels straight up the canal valley.

#### 7. Bodmin Moor edge — 9 miles, longer day walk (Grade C)

**A full day on the inland moor. Stone circles, granite tors, ponies. Drive 30 minutes from the estate.**

The walk we send people on when they want to escape the coast entirely and feel the old, granite, inland version of Cornwall. Bodmin Moor is twenty miles south-east of the estate — a 30-minute drive to Minions car park — and from there a circuit takes you over The Cheesewring (a 32-foot stack of weathered granite slabs balanced impossibly on a cliff edge), past the Bronze Age stone circle of The Hurlers (three concentric rings of standing stones, dating to roughly 1500 BC), and across open moorland grazed by the wild Bodmin ponies.

Big landscape. Buzzards, ravens, the occasional kestrel. In late August, the heather flowers in three colours — bell, ling, cross-leaved — and the moor turns purple from horizon to horizon. In November, the mist rolls down from Brown Willy (Cornwall's highest point at 420m, visible to the north) and you can lose all bearings in under a minute. Carry a map. Compass useful.

Surface: grass and granite. No stiles, but several stream crossings (wet feet likely after rain). Dogs welcome but on lead — ponies, sheep, and the moor's habit of swallowing things.

Drive: 30 minutes. Start what3words: ///shop.spend.darts. Pub at end: The Cheesewring Hotel, dog-friendly, food until 8pm. Allow six hours plus drive time.

#### 8. Crackington Haven coastal — 6 miles, challenging (Grade C)

**The earned walk. Six miles, six hundred feet of cliffs, the highest in mainland Cornwall.**

Crackington Haven is a ten-minute drive south down the coast. From the village car park, the SWCP climbs immediately and stays climbed. The cliffs here — High Cliff, named accurately — are the highest in mainland Cornwall, rising 731 feet straight out of the water. The path traces the contour just below the top, with the swell three hundred meters down on your left and grazed pasture above on your right.

This is the walk that justifies the deep bath afterwards. Six miles total, two hours of steady ascent with a long, beautiful traverse along the top, then a knee-grinding descent into Strangles Beach (a half-mile of dark sand and rock-pools, dog-friendly, often empty), and a return along the inland combe via Trevigue farm. Total ascent: 350m. Total stiles: six. Surface: cliff path, exposed throughout.

Big walk. Carry water. Carry layers. Carry a waterproof even if the forecast is clear — the cliff weather is its own jurisdiction. Dogs on lead the whole way, no exceptions: cliff edge unfenced, sheep on every other field, and the descent to Strangles is steep enough that a dog off-lead in front of you is a real risk.

Reward: the Coombe Barton Inn at the end of the walk, dog-friendly, log fire, fish-and-chips, and a perfectly poured local IPA. Drive: 12 minutes. Start what3words: ///wishbone.hopeful.cardboard. Allow four hours including beach stop.

## /estate/farm — Working Farm Story

### BLUF
The Whalesborough farm is a working mixed Cornish farm of 320 productive acres within the wider 500-acre estate. It produces lamb, free-range eggs, market-garden vegetables, honey from fifteen thousand Cornish Black Bees, and the cut flowers on the Weir restaurant tables. Tim Hutchings has managed the land for twenty-three years. Guests are welcome to walk it, watch it work, and book onto experiences from a £25 alpaca walk to a £450 day with Tim.

### The story — 800 words

There is a particular quality of Cornish light that arrives at about ten past six in the morning in June — low, sideways, the colour of unsalted butter — and Tim Hutchings is usually halfway through the lower paddock by the time it hits him. He has been the estate manager at Whalesborough for twenty-three years. He started here when the farm was 220 acres of tired pasture and a small flock of mongrel sheep, and he has, slowly and methodically, with the kind of pragmatism that does not photograph well but does build something real, made it into a 500-acre working estate that supplies its own restaurant, grows its own flowers, makes its own honey, and runs at a quiet profit.

This is the part of Whalesborough that the rebranding consultants gently suggest we hide. The diesel-stained quad bike. The third-shoulder of the polytunnel that has been patched with farm tape for eleven years. The fact that the lambing shed in March smells exactly the way you'd expect a lambing shed to smell. We don't hide it. The working farm is the spine the rest of the resort hangs off. The restaurant menu is shorter on Mondays because the polytunnel has its weekly day off. The breakfast eggs are warm because they came in from the hen house an hour ago. The salad has soil on it because Tim's daughter Mabel washes them in a bucket by the back door.

**The herd and flock.** Whalesborough runs a small flock of around 140 ewes — a cross of Cornish Mule and Suffolk, chosen for hardiness on the south-westerly weather and for the quality of the meat. Lambing is late February through to early April, and the lambs run with their mothers on the lake meadow until weaning in June. The pork comes from twelve resident Tamworth and Saddleback rare-breed pigs, who have a deliberately large outdoor run in the lower woodland. We also keep four Dexter cattle — a small native Irish breed — primarily for conservation grazing of the wildflower meadow.

The poultry yard runs about 80 free-range layers (a mix of Marans, Light Sussex, Cream Legbar and Speckledy), 30 ducks (Aylesbury, Khaki Campbell, and a half-dozen Indian Runners who patrol the polytunnel for slugs), and a small flock of geese who own the lake meadow and consider the rest of us guests.

**The market garden — Neetfield.** Twelve acres of intensive market garden a half-mile east of the main farm, run by head grower Eva Quintrell and a rotating team of three. We grow over forty varieties of vegetable, herb, salad and cut-flower across the season — Cornish purple sprouting broccoli, salad burnet, monk's beard, eight kinds of squash, climbing French beans, edible flowers, the lot. The kitchen places a Sunday-evening order for the week, and what arrives is what's at peak that Monday. This is the most direct piece of farm-to-fork practice in the resort and it explains the menu changes you'll see — they are not affectation, they are because the dahlia tubers finally came up.

**The bees.** We keep fifteen thousand Cornish Black Bees in twelve hives at the Neetfield apiary. The Cornish Black (*Apis mellifera mellifera*) is the native British honeybee — darker, smaller, and significantly more weather-tolerant than the imported Italian strains used elsewhere. It is also a Cornish conservation priority: the genetic line nearly died out in the 1990s and is being slowly restored by a small network of Cornish beekeepers, of which we are now part. The Whalesborough apiary produces around 200 jars of honey per year, served on the Weir breakfast and sold in the farm shop. Our beekeeping experiences (£45) and Beekeeper for a Day (£450) are run from this site.

**What we are not.** We are not a petting zoo. The animals are not here for guest entertainment — they are working livestock. There are scheduled feeding sessions (Tuesdays and Saturdays at 10:30am, free, RSVP) and a thirty-minute Little Farmers session for families with under-tens. Otherwise the animals are getting on with their lives. Children are absolutely welcome to watch from outside the gates. Dogs strictly out of all stock paddocks.

We are also not a glamping wellness retreat dressed up in farm aesthetic. The mud is real. The straw is real. The smell on a hot August day is real. Tim's hands are real. This matters.

## /estate/farm/animals — Resident Animals

### BLUF
The farm is home to roughly 280 resident animals across eight species — alpacas, goats, donkeys, pigs, chickens, ducks, sheep and bees. Two species (alpacas, donkeys) accept human contact daily. The rest are working livestock kept outdoors at proper densities. All animals can be observed during morning farm walks; some can be booked for closer experiences.

### Editorial intro — 300 words

There is a particular way that animals behave when they are not being managed for human entertainment, and you can read it in their bodies straight away. The pigs are asleep in the shade at one in the afternoon. The geese are not approaching anyone for food. The alpacas have ears forward and tails relaxed. The chickens are dust-bathing in the corner of the yard. The donkey, Maud, is doing what donkeys do, which is mostly standing.

This is what a working farm looks like at proper densities, and it is — counter-intuitively — what makes the farm so calming to be near. The animals are calibrated to their actual lives, not to footfall, not to feeding photos, not to small children with handfuls of pellets at the wrong end of the day. We do run scheduled contact sessions — Little Farmers on Tuesdays and Saturdays at 10:30, the alpaca walks twice a week, occasional pop-ups in school holidays — but those sessions are short, well-managed, and exist as a deliberate part of each animal's enrichment.

The rest of the time, the animals are doing their work: grazing meadows, fertilising soil, eating slugs, laying eggs, raising lambs, making honey. You are welcome to watch from the public paths and from the orchard bench. You are not welcome to feed them outside scheduled times — please don't.

The eight resident species are below. Each has its own page with the basic biology, our individual animal histories (yes, with names — Alberto and Alfonzo are real, the goats are not just "the goats"), the daily routine, and how to meet them properly.

Dogs strictly out of all paddocks. Children always supervised. Quiet voices. The farm thanks you.

### 1. Alpacas — Alberto and Alfonzo

Alberto (the dark fawn) and Alfonzo (the rose-grey) are Huacaya alpacas, born June 2019 in Camelford from a small Cornish breeder we have worked with for years. They came to Whalesborough as eighteen-month-old wethers (castrated males), which is the standard alpaca arrangement for working with humans — entire males develop an attitude that does not translate well to walks.

Alpacas are camelids. They are not sheep, they are not llamas, and they do not spit at people who treat them with respect. They are intelligent, calm, hum to each other when content, and process the world with a slightly bemused dignity that is impossible to dislike. They will let you walk with them — on lead, at their pace, with a halter you've fitted yourself — but they will not be hurried. The Alpaca Walk experience (£25 adult, £15 child, 6+, Wednesdays and Sundays at 2pm, max 8 walkers) is the most popular booked experience on the estate.

Alfonzo is the bossier one and will check you out first. Alberto is shyer and will follow Alfonzo's lead. Both have been hand-raised and are perfectly steady around children and dogs, though dogs must be at a respectful distance — the alpacas can outrun any dog you bring, but they shouldn't have to.

Diet: hay, grass, supplementary alpaca pellets. Shearing: annually in early June. Fleece: spun by a Cornish weaver in Liskeard, jumpers available in the farm shop.

### 2. Goats — the herd

Six goats: four Boer (the white-and-tan meat breed, here as conservation grazers for the steep bank above the lake), two pygmy goats (Beryl and Edna, who live in the goat pen by the farm gate and accept careful greetings).

Beryl is the bossy one. She is the size of a corgi and has more opinions than anyone you have ever met. She will climb the fence-line to inspect you, and she will accept a forehead scratch if you stand still long enough. Edna is more reserved. Both are aged about six and were rescued from a closing smallholding in Devon in 2021.

The four Boer goats are working stock. They are kept on the steep ground that the sheep can't easily graze, and they clear bramble and gorse to make space for wildflower regeneration. You can see them from the orchard bench, but the field is closed to walkers in the lambing/kidding window (March/April).

Goats are smarter than dogs and considerably more devious. Do not bring food into the pen. They will find it.

### 3. Donkeys — Maud and Gertie

Maud and Gertie are rescue donkeys, retired from a beach-ride operation in Devon and rehomed via The Donkey Sanctuary at Sidmouth. Maud is about twenty-four (donkeys live forty-plus), grey, calm, and the senior figure on the farm. Gertie is sixteen, dark brown, and Maud's constant companion — donkeys are pair-bonded animals and separating them is not done.

They live in the paddock by the visitor car park. They are happy to be greeted over the gate. They will not be hurried. They will, if you stand quietly for two minutes, come over and accept a soft palm-down stroke on the muzzle.

Do not feed them. Their diet is precisely managed — apple-and-carrot routes to a lot of preventable hoof and weight problems in retired donkeys. The farm team will tell you off, kindly.

Maud has been on three local radio interviews. Gertie is the more photogenic. Both are part of the Tuesday and Saturday Little Farmers sessions.

### 4. Pigs — the Tamworths and Saddlebacks

Twelve rare-breed pigs in the lower woodland. Eight Tamworth (the ginger ones, the oldest registered British breed, dating to before 1800) and four Saddleback (the black-with-white-belt ones, a cross of Wessex Saddleback and Essex). They live outdoors year-round in a deliberately large, wooded run of about three acres, with proper shelters, wallows, and a hill to dig.

This is honest pig farming. The pigs are reared slowly to about thirteen months, much longer than commercial pork, and the resulting meat (sold in the farm shop, served in the Weir restaurant) is dark, well-marbled, and tastes like pork actually used to taste. We have one batch in process at any time — there are no permanent named pet pigs here, and this is a deliberate decision.

You can see them from the woodland-walk path through the trees. Best time: late morning when they are usually rooting. Afternoon they sleep. Do not enter the run. Do not throw food over the fence — wrong diet, real harm.

Quiet observation only. Children: yes, with a hand-hold.

### 5. Chickens — the laying flock

Around eighty layers in a free-range flock, in a fox-proofed yard by the dairy. A mix of Marans (dark-brown egg layers, French breed), Light Sussex (white-and-black, traditional British), Cream Legbar (blue-egg layers, my personal favourite), and Speckledy (brown speckled feathers, brown eggs).

They produce roughly fifty-five eggs a day at peak, dropping to about twenty in midwinter, and supply the Weir breakfast service plus the farm shop. The Cream Legbar's pale-blue eggs are reserved for the restaurant. You can see them from the yard fence, and during the Tuesday/Saturday Little Farmers session, children can collect eggs with the team (eggs from clean nest boxes, gloves provided, supervised).

Foxes have got the flock twice in eight years — both times via dug holes under the wire. We have rebuilt with concrete-set wire. Vigilance remains.

Diet: layers' mash, kitchen vegetable scraps, free range across the dairy yard. Lifespan in a laying flock: 3–4 years productive, longer in retirement. Retired hens go to a local volunteer rehoming network — please ask if you are interested.

### 6. Ducks — the polytunnel patrol

Thirty ducks: ten Aylesbury (white, calm, traditional table duck), ten Khaki Campbell (the brown laying breed, prolific egg layers — about two hundred eggs per duck per year), and ten Indian Runners (the upright, comical ones, originally from Indonesia, kept here as slug-control in the polytunnel).

The Indian Runners are the showstoppers. They walk upright, in a tight flock, and travel between the polytunnel and the duck pond in single file. The market garden team open the polytunnel for them at sunrise; they spend the day eating slugs and snails (their preferred diet, far more effective than any chemical control), and they come home at sunset like commuters.

You can see them from the duck-pond bench by the orchard. The pond itself is fed by a spring and stays at about 12°C year-round. Ducks lay early-morning, so the eggs you have at breakfast were laid before five.

Dogs strictly away from the pond. The ducks are calm but will bolt, and a stress-bolted duck is a sad duck. No feeding.

### 7. Sheep — the flock

About 140 ewes plus their lambs in season. A cross of Cornish Mule (a Bluefaced Leicester ram on a Hardy Speckled-Face ewe) and Suffolk, chosen for hardiness on the wet, windy south-westerly climate and for the eating quality of the lamb.

The flock rotates through the estate's grazing land in a five-paddock conservation rotation — sheep on a meadow for three weeks, off for ten, allowing the wildflowers to set seed. This system is what produces the wildflower density you'll see in the lake meadow in May and June. It is also why some fields are closed to walking at certain times — please respect the waymarkers.

Lambing is late February through early April, indoors in the lambing shed with 24-hour shepherds (Tim does the night shifts; he is functionally nocturnal for six weeks). The shed is closed to guests during this period for the obvious reason that exhausted ewes do not need an audience. From late April, the lambs are out in the meadows and you will see them everywhere.

Dogs strictly on lead in any sheep paddock. A loose dog around lambs is a real and lawful danger to the flock; farmers in Cornwall have the right to shoot dogs that worry livestock and we very much do not want to be involved in that conversation.

### 8. Bees — the 15,000 Cornish Blacks

Twelve hives at the Neetfield apiary, holding approximately fifteen thousand worker bees per active hive plus drones, brood and a single queen. The Cornish Black Bee (*Apis mellifera mellifera*) is the native British honeybee subspecies — darker, hairier and smaller than the more common imported Italian bees, with significantly better cold-and-wet tolerance.

The Cornish Black was nearly lost in the 1990s — varroa mite, imported queens, and a long decline in beekeeping skills combined to wipe out 90% of the population. A small network of Cornish beekeepers, working with the Cornish Black Bee Project, has been slowly restoring the genetic line. Whalesborough is part of that network. Our queens are bred from local stock and we do not import.

The apiary produces about 200 jars of honey a year — single-flowering-source where we can (heather, bramble, lime, sweet chestnut), polyfloral for the rest. Served on the Weir breakfast table, sold in the farm shop, and used in the spa's signature Honey Drift treatment.

You can see the apiary from the Bee Walk eco-education trail (with an interpretation panel at fifty feet — closer is not advisable without a suit). Guests can book the Beekeeping Experience (£45, includes full suit, two hours with the head beekeeper, May to September only, ages 12+). Premium-tier guests can book Beekeeper for a Day (£450, four hours with Tim, lunch with the head beekeeper, one jar of honey to take home, by appointment only, 14+).

Do not approach the apiary without a suit, even from a distance you think is safe. Bees orient on movement and scent. They are calm in calm conditions, less so in storm-fronts.

### BLUF
North Cornwall — Bude, Marhamchurch, Crackington Haven, Boscastle, Tintagel — is the destination, not generic Cornwall. ### Editorial intro — 400 words

This is north Cornwall — the bit most maps cut off at the top of the page, the coastline that runs from Bude to Hartland Point with practically nothing built on it for fifteen miles in either direction. The cliffs are higher here than anywhere in mainland Cornwall (731 feet at High Cliff above Crackington Haven, the official tallest), the surf rolls in straight from three thousand miles of open Atlantic, and the towns are small, working, and largely undeveloped. There is no airport within ninety minutes. There is no motorway within fifty. There is, on the whole, less of everything except weather.

This matters because the rest of Cornwall — Padstow, Newquay, St Ives — has been comprehensively packaged. The food is excellent, the cottages are renovated, the parking is queued from 9am. North Cornwall has not gone the same way. The pubs still serve pies on a Tuesday lunchtime. The Sea Pool at Summerleaze is still free. The Brendon Arms still has the same landlord it had in 2008. The light, in the second half of August, is still the colour it has always been — silvered, sideways, sometimes ferocious, occasionally exactly the colour of the Heinz mushroom soup label.

We are two and a half miles inland from Bude — a working market town with a proper high street, two great independent bookshops, six pubs and a sea-lock that is the only operational canal sea-entrance in the country. Beyond Bude, the destinations in this guide are organised by drive-time: under ten minutes for Widemouth Bay, Summerleaze, and Crooklets; ten to twenty for Crackington Haven and Hartland Quay; twenty to thirty for Boscastle and Tintagel; thirty-plus for Port Isaac, Padstow, and the inland Bodmin Moor.

Be honest with yourself about access. North Cornwall is a place you visit with a car. The bus network exists but runs on a frequency designed for school children and the weekly shop. ### 1. Widemouth Bay — 5 miles, surf, family

**BLUF.** Widemouth Bay is a half-mile stretch of golden sand and rolling Atlantic surf, five miles south of the estate. Two car parks (north and south), three surf schools, a beach café, lifeguards in season, and the most consistent surf break on this stretch of coast. **Sensory intro.** The first thing you notice is the size of the sky. Widemouth opens out at the end of a steep, narrow lane and the bay itself is enormous — a long, shallow curve of pale sand with the cliff line dropping away to the north and the rocky reef of Black Rock guarding the southern end. The surf rolls in clean and steady almost every day of the year, breaking white about a hundred metres out. On a southerly, the wind blows offshore and the wave faces hold up beautifully. ### 3. Crooklets Beach — 3 miles, family

### 4. Bude town — 2 miles

### 6. Boscastle — 16 miles, harbour

**BLUF.** Boscastle is a small fishing harbour 16 miles south on the SWCP, with a steep-sided natural inlet protected by two stone breakwaters. Famous for the 2004 flood; rebuilt since. The 2004 flood killed nobody but destroyed half the village; the rebuild has been done well, and the National Trust visitor centre tells the story with restraint. ### 7. Tintagel — 20 miles, Castle

**BLUF.** Tintagel Castle is an English Heritage ruin on a dramatic clifftop island 20 miles south, reached by a 70-metre-high steel footbridge (opened 2019). The site is the largest body of post-Roman archaeology in Britain, with strong evidence of a 5th–7th century royal seat. The King Arthur association is later medieval marketing. The geology and the view alone are worth the day. Dogs welcome on lead.

**Sensory intro.** The road to Tintagel village winds for twenty minutes through high hedgerow lanes and drops you in a small, cluttered tourist village that has very little to offer beyond the Castle. Walk past the King Arthur's Hall, past the tat shops, past the Cornish Pasty stand — and then the cliff path opens out and the Castle itself appears on the headland: a roofless ruin of medieval stone perched on the very tip of an island connected to the mainland by a narrow, vertiginous neck of rock.

Since 2019, the two halves are linked by a 70-metre-high steel footbridge that floats just above the original mediaeval bridge line. The bridge alone is worth the visit — open metalwork, no glass floor, with the swell crashing 200 feet below on both sides. Cross it slowly. The Castle ruin is fragmentary but the views across the bay (Tintagel Haven below, Lundy Island on a clear day to the north) are stunning. The 13th-century walls are real and impressive. The Arthurian story is largely a 12th-century literary invention by Geoffrey of Monmouth, embellished by the Victorians, and amplified by English Heritage's marketing — we say this plainly because the cliché is annoying. The genuine story is the 5th–7th century royal site, which is much more interesting and far less hyped.

**Dog rules.** Dogs welcome on lead on the mainland approach. Not permitted on the island itself (cliff drop, archaeology). Welcome at Tintagel village pubs.

**Accessibility.** The bridge crossing is fully accessible. The island itself is steep, with mostly steps. A Land Rover ferry shuttle runs (free) for less mobile visitors to the island access point. EH membership recommended (£18 adult day pass without).

**Parking.** Tintagel village car park: £6 day, 200 spaces, fills 11am summer. Cliff car park (closer, EH members): £4 day.

**Family suitability.** Good for school-age kids interested in castles, ghosts, or steep drops. Under-fives find the bridge frightening. Teen-friendly — the photography opportunities are excellent.

**Best time.** Late afternoon, last admission at 4pm summer, fewer crowds. Off-season the village empties and the Castle is at its best. Stormy days are spectacular but bridge sometimes closed in high wind.

**Distance from Whalesborough.** 40 minutes by car, 20 miles. By public transit: bus 595 (1.5 hours, twice daily summer). Realistic only by car. We offer a guided half-day to Tintagel through this app (£55, Wednesdays and Saturdays — see Activities).

### 8. Port Isaac — 25 miles, fishing

**BLUF.** Port Isaac is a small working fishing village 25 miles south, famous as the filming location for ITV's *Doc Martin* and the home of the Fisherman's Friends sea-shanty group. A tight, steep cluster of whitewashed cottages around a working harbour, with two Michelin-starred restaurants (Nathan Outlaw's New Road and Outlaw's Fish Kitchen). Busy in summer. **Sensory intro.** Port Isaac is small. The village clings to a steep combe that drops to a tiny working harbour, with the streets so narrow in places that cars cannot pass each other. From the cliff car park you walk down for ten minutes through stone cottages and reach the harbour proper — a half-moon of pebbles and slipway with fishing boats hauled up above the high-water mark and lobster pots stacked at the back. The smell, on a working day, is unmistakeably fish and salt and tarred rope.

The *Doc Martin* connection has made Port Isaac globally famous and uncomfortably busy in summer (May to September, expect tour buses). The Fisherman's Friends — the sea-shanty group who sing on the harbour wall on summer Friday evenings — are a real local tradition, not a tourist invention; they have been singing on that wall since 1995. The two Outlaw restaurants are world-class (Nathan Outlaw's New Road in particular — book months ahead). ### 9. Padstow + Rock — ~32 miles

**BLUF.** Padstow is the major fishing port on the Camel Estuary, 32 miles south, with the largest concentration of high-end seafood restaurants in the southwest (Rick Stein's empire of four restaurants plus the deli and seafood school, plus Paul Ainsworth at No. 6). The harbour is busy with working boats and tourist boats and hire boats, the streets are tight and full, and from May to September it is genuinely heaving. But the food is exceptional — Rick Stein's Seafood Restaurant has been running since 1975, Paul Ainsworth's No. 6 holds a Michelin star, the Cornish Arms is a perfectly proper village pub.

The ferry across the Camel Estuary to Rock (a small adult charge, runs every fifteen minutes April to October) is the single most pleasant boat ride in Cornwall — five minutes across the estuary mouth, looking out to the Atlantic on one side and inland up the river on the other. Rock itself is quieter, with a long sandy beach (the famous Rock Beach), the Mariner's pub, and the start of the Camel Trail — eighteen miles of flat, dog-friendly, family-friendly converted railway line all the way to Bodmin.

### 11. Hartland Quay — 28 miles

**BLUF.** Hartland Quay is one of the most dramatic and remote points on the entire SWCP — a small, isolated 17th-century quay at the foot of a 200-foot cliff, with the iconic folded-rock formations of north Devon stretching for miles in both directions. One pub-hotel (the Hartland Quay Hotel), one museum, one tiny beach. No bus service. Car essential. Worth the drive for cliff-walkers and geologists. Dogs welcome.

**Sensory intro.** Hartland Quay is the most spectacular bit of coastline in southern Britain and you have to genuinely commit to get there. The drive from the estate runs forty minutes inland through the Hartland peninsula, past the village of Hartland, down a long, narrow lane that ends in a tiny clifftop car park. From there, the cliff drops away to a small stone quay (built 1593, mostly destroyed in the 1880s, partially rebuilt) and a beach of dark, water-smoothed rock. The cliffs to either side are folded into vertical creases — the famous Hartland geology, the Carboniferous strata buckled up by tectonic forces 300 million years ago — and look unlike anywhere else in Britain.

The Hartland Quay Hotel sits at the top of the cliff, with rooms that overhang the sea and a bar that has been serving sailors and writers since 1825. The food is honest. The pints are cold. The view from the terrace is, frankly, one of the great views in England.

**Dog rules.** Dogs welcome throughout. Hartland Quay Hotel is dog-friendly. SWCP both directions: on lead at cliff edges.

**Accessibility.** The cliff descent to the quay itself is steep, with concrete steps. Not wheelchair-accessible. The hotel and the cliff-top terrace are accessible. The car park has no facilities beyond the hotel.

**Parking.** Hartland Quay car park: £5 day, 40 spaces, never fills.

**Family suitability.** Excellent for older children (10+) with an interest in geology, dramatic landscapes, or storm-watching. Younger children find the descent steep and the wind exposed. The Hartland Quay Hotel does proper child-friendly pub food.

**Best time.** Sunset in late summer is genuinely incredible (the sun drops into the sea over Lundy Island). A south-westerly storm in October is the great storm-watching experience of British coastline. Calm summer days are also lovely.

**Distance from Whalesborough.** 50 minutes by car, 28 miles. By public transit: not viable. The nearest bus runs to Hartland village (3 miles inland) twice a week and there is no onward connection. Drive, or arrange a private transfer (Bude Taxis, approximately £50 each way).

### 12. Bodmin Moor — ~20 miles

**BLUF.** Bodmin Moor is the largest area of upland moor in Cornwall — 80 square miles of granite, heather, ancient stone circles, wild ponies, and high inland landscape. Approximately 30 minutes' drive south-east, with multiple access points. Recommended starting points: Minions (for The Cheesewring and The Hurlers stone circle), Brown Willy (Cornwall's highest point at 420m), and Dozmary Pool (Arthurian connection, atmospheric). Dogs welcome. Car essential.

**Sensory intro.** Bodmin Moor is the inland version of Cornwall — the part most coastal visitors never see — and it is one of the most atmospheric landscapes in southern Britain. Twenty miles inland from the estate, the moor rises out of the surrounding farmland as a high, granite plateau covered in coarse grass, bracken, gorse, and heather. The famous landmarks — The Cheesewring (a 32-foot stack of weathered granite slabs), The Hurlers (three concentric rings of Bronze Age standing stones, c. 1500 BC), Dozmary Pool (the legendary final resting place of Excalibur), Brown Willy (Cornwall's highest point at 420m) — are scattered across it like punctuation.

The wild Bodmin Moor ponies (a small, hardy native breed) graze the open moor year-round and you will see them within minutes of getting out of the car. Buzzards are common, ravens too, and the occasional kestrel. In late August the heather flowers in three overlapping species — bell, ling, cross-leaved — and the entire moor turns purple. In November and February, the mist rolls in fast and a compass becomes essential. The Beast of Bodmin (the alleged big cat) is a fun local legend with no credible evidence; the wild ponies are real and worth seeing.

**Dog rules.** Dogs welcome throughout but on lead — wild ponies and grazing sheep everywhere.

**Accessibility.** Minions car park is on a metalled road; the Cheesewring walk is a 1.5-mile rough track each way, not wheelchair-suitable. Some moor edges have hard-surface paths. The moor as a whole is rough underfoot.

**Parking.** Multiple — Minions village car park (free, 50 spaces), Bolventor (for Jamaica Inn — yes, the Daphne du Maurier setting, real pub, dog-friendly, food, gift shop), Dozmary Pool (small layby). EV charging at Jamaica Inn.

**Family suitability.** Good for school-age kids interested in standing stones, ponies, or general adventure. Less interesting for under-fives, who find the distances long.

**Best time.** Late August for the purple heather. April–May for the moor in bloom. Clear winter days for the long views (Brown Willy can see both coasts on the right day). Avoid heavy mist days without proper navigation.

**Distance from Whalesborough.** 35 minutes by car, 20 miles. By public transit: not viable. Drive essential.

### Dog-Friendly Cornwall — The Honest Guide

**BLUF.** Cornwall is one of the most dog-friendly counties in England, and north Cornwall in particular has more year-round dog-welcoming beaches, more dog-friendly pubs per capita, and more accessible coast path than almost anywhere else in the country. Whalesborough Farm Resort & Spa sits at the centre of this — a 500-acre estate with dedicated off-lead meadows, plumbed water bowls at every gate, the only canine wash bay in Cornwall's holiday parks, and immediate access to four year-round dog beaches within ten miles. This guide is what we actually tell guests, based on actual visits.

**The full text — 1,200 words**

Cornwall has roughly three categories of dog-welcoming holiday: the resort that says "pets allowed" in fine print and means it tolerates you with a £40 supplement; the cottage that means dogs are properly welcome but offers nothing dog-specific; and the small handful of estates and lodges that have actually thought about the dog as a guest. Whalesborough is in the last category. We have built around dogs because 23–46% of our guests bring one, because the founder's family has always travelled with dogs, and because Cornwall makes no sense as a destination if the dog has to be left at home.

This is the practical, year-round, weather-tested guide.

**On the estate.** All Whalesborough accommodation accepts dogs (no breed restrictions, no weight limit, no per-night surcharge above £25). Two dedicated off-lead dog meadows. Twenty-eight plumbed water bowls across the estate. A canine wash-bay at the farm gate with hot water, dog shampoo, towels, and a hairdryer — the only one of its kind in any Cornish holiday park (Landal removed theirs in 2024; we kept ours and added a second). Doggy welcome packs in every cottage: blanket, two dishes, towel, a Cornish dog biscuit, a tide-times card. Dog-friendly outdoor seating at The Weir restaurant (a third of the covers).

**Beaches.** Cornwall has a complicated beach-dog regime: most beaches ban dogs from Easter to October 1, but a generous handful are dog-friendly year-round. Within ten miles of Whalesborough, the year-round dog-welcome beaches are:

*Black Rock Beach* (10 minutes south of Bude, 7 miles from estate). Rocky, dramatic, rock-pools at low tide, never busy. Dogs always.

*Northcott Mouth Beach* (5 miles north of estate). Sand-and-rock, dramatic cliffs, surf, often empty mid-week. Dogs always.

*Millook Haven* (over 10 miles south). Large pebbles, dramatic V-cliff, quiet because the lane in is single-track and parking is limited (12 spaces). Dogs always. Best at low tide.

*Sandy Mouth Beach* (4 miles north, National Trust). Beautiful, popular, dogs banned in the central section in summer only — the south end and the cliff path are dog-friendly all year.

For the seasonal beaches (Summerleaze, Crooklets, Widemouth, Bude), dogs are excluded from Easter to October 1 in the busy sections. We have the year-round map in this app, refreshed quarterly with any rule changes from the local councils.

**Walks.** The estate is a five-minute walk from the Bude Canal towpath (flat, hard-packed gravel, dog-friendly throughout, lead recommended for cyclists). The towpath connects to the South West Coast Path at the sea-lock, and from there you have 630 miles of mostly dog-welcome coast path. Dog rules on the SWCP are simple: lead through any field with stock, recall-only off-lead on cliff path sections, and stay back from cliff edges (the wind off the Atlantic regularly gusts to 40 mph here and an excited dog is a real edge risk). The eight signed walks from the estate are detailed on the Walks page.

**Pubs.** Five dog-welcoming pubs within twenty minutes of the estate, all of which serve dogs water on arrival and most of which have dog biscuits at the bar.

*The Bay View Inn, Widemouth Bay* — 5 miles. Clifftop, dramatic sunset, dog-friendly throughout including the restaurant. Good seafood.

*The Brendon Arms, Bude* — 2 miles. Canalside, big garden, dog-friendly throughout, live music Friday nights.

*The Coombe Barton Inn, Crackington Haven* — 10 miles. Dog-friendly throughout, woodburner, fish-and-chips, the proper post-walk experience.

*The Cobweb Inn, Boscastle* — 16 miles. Ancient (1538), dog-friendly throughout, real ales, history-laden.

For dog-friendly cafés, The Beach Hut Café at Crooklets and Tom's Tea Garden at Summerleaze both welcome dogs at outdoor tables. Most Bude high-street cafés are dog-friendly inside as well.

**Vet, emergency, and on-resort.** Our concierge holds an out-of-hours vet number (Pet Doctors, Bude, 24/7 emergency line) and we can arrange same-day appointments. Doggy day-care is available on the estate by arrangement with our team (subject to vaccination certificates and a 15-minute meet-and-greet). Dog-walking service by arrangement (£15 for a one-hour walk by an estate team member). The wash-bay is free to all staying guests.

**What to bring (the honest list).** Two leads (one short for crowded paths, one extending for the off-lead meadow). A tide-times card (we provide). A towel (we also provide, but you'll want your own). Dog poo bags (compostable, available free at every gate). A blanket for the car after a wet walk. Water bowl (we provide one in every cottage). A muzzle if your dog is sheep-curious — we don't ask why, we just provide if you don't have one.

**Tide times matter.** Cornish beaches drown twice a day. Black Rock Beach at high tide is six feet of cliff edge — at low tide it's two hundred metres of rock pools and sand. Always check the tide before you go: we have it live in this app, refreshed every fifteen minutes for the eight nearest beaches.

**A note on sheep.** The Cornish countryside is mostly working farmland. Sheep and lambs are everywhere — particularly March to June, and August to October. Sheep-worrying by dogs is the single biggest preventable wildlife problem in this county, and Cornish farmers have full legal authority to shoot a dog actively chasing livestock. We have never had this happen with a Whalesborough guest's dog, and we'd very much like to keep it that way. If your dog is sheep-curious, lead at all times in any stock paddock. If your recall is uncertain, lead. If you don't know, lead. The lead-rules waymarkers across the estate are not advisory.

**A note on rip currents and dog rescue.** Don't enter the sea after your dog. Cornish beaches have proper rip currents that have killed humans trying to retrieve pets. Lifeguards are present at Summerleaze, Crooklets and Widemouth in season; outside season, call 999 and ask for the Coastguard. Dogs survive surprisingly well in the sea on their own; humans do not.

**Honest finally.** Cornwall is a great dog holiday and Whalesborough is a great dog base, but it is not Disneyland for dogs. The estate is a working farm. The sheep are real. The cliffs are real. The Atlantic does not negotiate. With proper precautions — lead in stock paddocks, recall in the off-lead meadow, common sense at cliff edges, tide-times checked, vet number saved — your dog will have the best week of its life.

### Family Holiday Cornwall — The Real Itinerary

**BLUF.** North Cornwall is the best family corner of the southwest because it is the part that has not been over-engineered. The beaches are real, the towns are working, the coast path is free, the Sea Pool at Summerleaze is free, the estate is a working farm with daily animal sessions for under-tens. We have built a family-graded itinerary across the under-five, five-to-eleven, and twelve-plus age bands, based on what we have actually done with our own families and what our guests genuinely come back for.

**The full text — 1,200 words**

A family holiday in Cornwall, done badly, is a queue of car parks and ice-cream-flavoured arguments at one in the afternoon. A family holiday done properly is the reverse: it is small, slow, weather-flexible, and built around what each age band actually enjoys. This is how we plan it for our own families and how we recommend it to our guests.

**The under-fives.** This age does not need an itinerary. It needs sand, a paddling-depth wave, a flask, an apple, and not much else. Bude Canal (flat, buggy-friendly, ducks). The Bude Castle heritage centre (small, free, indoor, two interactive rooms). The Bullers Arms in Marhamchurch (1-mile pram-walk from the estate, family-friendly Sunday lunch, garden). That is honestly enough for a week.

**The five-to-elevens.** This is the productive age. Long beach days, big cliff walks, rock-pooling, the surf school, the alpaca walk, the farm. The five-day rhythm we recommend:

*Day 1 — settle in.* Estate morning, Little Farmers, lake-walk, picnic at the orchard bench. Pool in the afternoon. Pizza in the cottage.

*Day 2 — Bude town day.* Walk in via the canal towpath (45 minutes, dogs and bikes welcome). Sea Pool swim, sandcastle on Summerleaze, fish-and-chips at the harbour. Bude Castle heritage centre if it rains.

*Day 4 — Crooklets rock-pools.* Best low-tide rock-pooling on the coast. Beach Hut Café doughnuts. Walk back via the cliff path to Summerleaze (15 minutes, easy).

*Day 5 — Tintagel.* The clifftop bridge, the castle ruins, the cliff view, the village. English Heritage day pass (or free with membership). Lunch at the King Arthur's Arms (dog-friendly, family-friendly). 40-minute drive each way; we run a guided half-day if you want someone else to drive.

The alpaca walk (Wednesday and Sunday afternoons, £15 child) is a peak experience for this age band. The Wild Kids forage in school holidays is free with RSVP and consistently the most-rebooked activity. The Halloween pumpkin trail in October half-term is genuinely magical.

**The twelve-pluses.** This age either bores easily or wants to do everything. The mix that works:

*Adventure.* Surf school every other day (Raven, Widemouth). Stand-up paddleboarding on the Bude Canal (£40, 12+). The wild sea swim (£35, 16+ only). Coast-path walks — Phillips Point, Crackington Haven coastal, Hartland day. Bike hire and the Camel Trail.

*Independence.* Twelve-pluses get to walk to Bude on their own (15 minutes from the estate gate, flat, dog-friendly, easy to find), have lunch in Bude, take the bus back. This is a small, real, much-appreciated freedom.

*Quiet experiences.* The photography walk (Friday morning, £30). The beekeeping experience (£45, 12+). The estate gin tasting (... no, not until 18, but the wreath-making and the Chef's Table are 12+).

*Wet-weather.* The Witchcraft Museum at Boscastle (8+ recommended). The Tate at St Ives (1 hour 40 minutes, day trip). The Newquay Aquarium (1 hour). The cinema in Bude (small, central, dog-friendly Friday matinees).

**Wet days, plural.** Cornwall will have wet days. Plan for three out of seven in the shoulder seasons. Wet-day options we have actually used:

*On estate.* The indoor pool (open 7am to 9pm, family swim 11–5, lifeguarded). The wellness garden marquee (storytelling sessions in school holidays, ad hoc). The library lounge in the W Club (jigsaws, board games, books). Wreath-making in December.

*Off estate.* Bude Castle (small, free, indoor). The Witchcraft Museum at Boscastle (paid, half-day, atmospheric). The Tate St Ives (paid, full day with the drive). Indoor mini-golf at the Bude leisure centre (£8 per round, school holidays). The cinema at Bude.

**The big mistakes parents make in Cornwall.** Five things we see repeatedly:

*Trying to do everything in one day.* North Cornwall is small but the roads are slow. Plan one major thing per day. Build in nap-time, ice-cream time, mope-around-the-cottage time.

*Not checking tides.* Beach plans live or die by the tide chart. Black Rock at high tide is rocks. Summerleaze rock-pools at high tide do not exist. Always check.

*Underestimating the weather.* The cliff path on a sunny morning is the most beautiful thing in England. The cliff path in a horizontal Atlantic squall, with a tired child, is not. Carry a real waterproof for each person. Always.

*Forgetting the wetsuit.* The sea here is 12°C in May and 16°C in August. A wetsuit makes the difference between a child enjoying the sea and a child enduring it. Wetsuit hire at Raven Surf (£10 day) or, better, buy a kid's wetsuit for the holiday (£40 from Bude Surf Shop, lasts three years).

*Booking ferries and restaurants too late.* In Padstow, Outlaw's books out four months ahead. The Padstow ferry doesn't book at all (turn up). The Tintagel bridge doesn't book (turn up). For Padstow, plan ahead.

**The honest list of what you need.** Wellies for every age. Waterproof for every age. A wetsuit per child if doing more than the occasional paddle. Buckets and spades (the cottage has some). Sun cream (the wind makes the sun feel less hot than it is — burn rate is high). A frisbee. A football. A small first-aid kit. The estate has a stocked pharmacy at the W Club for everything else.

Everything else, including the kettle, the highchair, the cot, the steriliser, the stair gate, and the dog water bowl, the cottage will already have.

### Spa & Wellness Cornwall — Where to Go, Honestly

**BLUF.** Cornwall has six genuine spa destinations, of which two — the W Club at Whalesborough, and the Bedruthan Hotel near Mawgan Porth — are worth a meaningful trip. The rest are good hotel spas with limited treatment depth. This guide compares them honestly, distinguishes spa-day from spa-stay, and explains why the W Club's "estate-to-treatment" programme (botanicals grown half a mile from the treatment room, single-source Cornish Black Bee honey, locally-blended oils) makes it the most editorially defensible wellness offer in the southwest.

**The full text — 1,200 words**

The spa world in the UK divides cleanly into three categories: the city-day-spa (Heckfield, Soho House, urban tier), the destination-stay (Coworth Park, Cliveden, country house), and the working-estate-spa (Daylesford, The Newt, Whalesborough). The third category is the most interesting and the rarest. It is also the hardest to do well because the storytelling depends on real provenance — actual botanicals grown on the actual land, served at the actual treatment table — and most operators take shortcuts.

Cornwall has, in our honest assessment, six spas worth visiting and two that are truly world-class. We work with three of them as referral partners and we visit the others regularly. This is the editorial map.

**The W Club at Whalesborough.** Our own spa. We have built it as an estate-to-treatment offer: the botanicals in our signature oils (lavender, rosemary, calendula) are grown by Eva at the Neetfield market garden half a mile from the treatment rooms. The honey in the Honey Drift treatment comes from our own apiary (fifteen thousand Cornish Black Bees, twelve hives, single-source single-flower wherever possible). The seaweed in the algae-wrap is harvested fortnightly by a Cornish forager from a single bay 12 miles south. The treatment menu is organised as Rituals (90 minutes, multi-modal) rather than a services list, and includes a one-hour Coast Walk integrated with the treatment for guests who want the full body-mind decompression. Pool: 25m, 29°C, UV-treated, indoor. Outdoor pool: 30°C in summer, southerly aspect. Sauna, steam, hot tub. The unique offer is the Spa for One (£950, 1–2 guests, sole-use 90 minutes, the only sole-use spa booking in Cornwall).

**The Bedruthan Hotel Spa, Mawgan Porth (1 hour 15 mins south).** The best comparable spa in north Cornwall. Beautifully clifftop, two pools (one infinity), Aveda treatment menu, family-friendly during the day, adults-only after 7pm. Not a working estate so no botanical provenance story. Strong on coastal mindfulness sessions and yoga retreats.

**The Headland Spa, Newquay (1 hour 30 mins south).** Big, capable, Aspar treatment menu, Victorian-hotel setting. Suits longer stays. Coastal but not editorially distinct.

**The Carbis Bay Hotel Spa (2 hours south, near St Ives).** Famously hosted the 2021 G7 summit. The spa is good, the location (its own private beach) is spectacular. Far from Bude.

**The Scarlet Hotel Spa, Mawgan Porth (1 hour 15 mins).** Adults-only, eco-credentialled, beautiful cliff-edge cedarwood reed-bed pool. Strong on outdoor cold-water and treatment-with-landscape sessions. Closer to a retreat than a hotel spa.

**The Watergate Bay Hotel Spa, Newquay (1 hour 30 mins).** Family-orientated, decent treatments, three-mile beach immediately outside. Suits families wanting a half-day spa break.

**What makes Cornwall a real spa destination.** Three things: the air, the water, and the bothering-with-provenance culture.

*The air* — Cornwall's prevailing wind is south-westerly across 3,000 miles of open Atlantic, which makes the air some of the cleanest measurable in Britain. The negative-ion count is genuinely high. Anyone with respiratory sensitivity notices it within hours.

*The water* — both the Atlantic and the inland (the canal, the lake) are cold and clean and accessible. Wild-swimming culture is real here, embedded, and there is a year-round community in Bude that has been swimming the Sea Pool every morning at 7am since the 1950s.

*The provenance culture* — Cornish growers, foragers, beekeepers, distillers and oil-blenders are some of the most developed in Britain. Daylesford set the standard nationally for estate-grown botanicals; in Cornwall, the equivalent network is smaller but tighter. Buyable. Visitable.

**Spa-day versus spa-stay versus spa-week.** Three different propositions and they require different planning.

*Spa-day* — three to four hours, one treatment, pool access. £150–£220 depending on treatment. Best for couples without children, or for a treat day during a holiday. Most Cornish spas sell these in 11am–3pm or 2pm–6pm slots, and the W Club runs them every weekday plus Saturday.

*Spa-stay* — two-night minimum, two treatments, daily wellness sessions, breakfast and dinner in the spa restaurant. £550–£950 per person depending on grade. The W Club's Trelowen and Gwari spa lodges (private hot tub, treatment-room booking, breakfast hampers) are our equivalent.

*Spa-week* — five to seven nights, programmed wellness, group classes, nutrition consultation, integrated body-mind work. The Scarlet runs a small handful of these per year; the Bedruthan runs themed wellness weeks; the W Club is launching a five-day Cornish Retreat in spring 2027.

**Five things to think about before booking a Cornish spa.**

*Treatment menu structure.* "Services" lists (massage 60, massage 90, body wrap, manicure) are operational thinking. "Rituals" or "journeys" are editorial thinking — multi-modal sequences with a narrative. The W Club, Daylesford, The Newt and Heckfield all use the latter. Trust this distinction.

*Pool culture.* Adult-only hours matter. The W Club is family-pool until 6pm, adult-only 6pm–9pm. The Bedruthan is family-day, adult-evening. The Scarlet is adult-only always. Plan around your party.

*Treatment provenance.* The honest test: ask where the seaweed/honey/oil/wax comes from. If the answer is a brand name (Aveda, Aspar, Elemis, ESPA), that is a global manufacturer. If the answer is a place (Whalesborough apiary, Cape Cornwall foraging boat, Helebridge essential oils blender), that is provenance. Both can be great; the provenance answer correlates with a more editorial experience.

*Booking lead-time.* Weekend afternoon slots are gone six weeks ahead from May through October. Mid-week mornings can be booked 48 hours ahead. The W Club's Spa for One is gone four months ahead in summer.

*Combine with what.* A spa day at the W Club pairs beautifully with the Weir Chef's Table (first Friday of the month, £95) for a true evening-out — treatment 2-5pm, drinks in the lounge, dinner 7pm. Best one-day pairing in Cornwall.

**On wild-swimming, properly.** The cold-water and wild-swimming scene around Bude is one of the strongest in southern Britain. The Sea Pool at Summerleaze is the spiritual centre — open year-round, free, lifeguarded in summer, with a strong dawn-swim community in winter. The W Club runs guided wild swims (£35 with dryrobe loan) every Wednesday April to October at dawn and sunset. The therapy is real: vagus-nerve stimulation, dopamine boost, the genuine post-swim afterglow. The risks are real too: cold shock, swim failure, rip currents. Always go guided your first time. Always tow-floated. Always with a hot drink waiting.

### Sustainable Luxury Cornwall — The Verifiable Operators

**BLUF.** "Sustainable luxury" in Cornwall is now a loaded phrase. Most operators claiming the label run two pieces of solar PV, an eco-bag at check-in, and a charcoal towel. The real category — operators with verifiable, third-party-audited sustainability — numbers about a dozen in the county. Whalesborough is in this category: Cleaner Seas Project microplastic filters, on-site wind turbine, biomass boiler, 50 acres of new woodland planted since 2019, single-source Cornish Black Bee restoration. This guide is the honest comparison, with the questions to ask, the certifications to look for, and what to ignore.

**The full text — 1,200 words**

There are now two Cornwalls in the sustainability conversation. There is the marketing Cornwall, where every operator's website features the word "sustainable" within the first hundred words, where most have a green leaf icon and a paragraph about reusable water bottles, and where the actual carbon-and-water-and-waste data is invisible. And there is the real Cornwall, where about a dozen operators are doing third-party-audited, measurable, verifiable work, and where the documentation is available on request.

This guide is the honest map.

**The four pillars of verifiable sustainability.** Skip past anything that doesn't measurably impact one of these.

*Carbon footprint.* On-site renewable generation (PV, wind, biomass, ground-source heat). Measurable carbon emissions per guest-night. Public progress reports. Net-zero by a published deadline. The strongest operators have ISO 14001 certification or Cornwall Sustainable Tourism (CoaST) Gold accreditation.

*Water and microplastics.* On-site greywater recycling. Microplastic filtration on the laundry outflow (this is the Cleaner Seas Project — almost no operators in the UK have this; we are one of the few). Rainwater capture for non-potable use. Native-species drought-tolerant planting.

*Biodiversity.* Land in regenerative management. New woodland planted. Bee-conservation work. Bird-and-bat habitat. Hedgerow restoration. The strongest land-managers have measurable species-count progress over five years.

*Local economy.* Percentage of staff hired locally (within 10 miles). Percentage of supply chain spent in Cornwall. Direct producer relationships (not wholesaler intermediaries). Living-wage employer accreditation.

**The verifiable operators in Cornwall (May 2026).** This is the list we use. Names included only where we have personally verified the claims via site visit, conversation with the operator, and a look at the published documentation.

*Whalesborough Farm Resort & Spa* (Bude, our own). Cleaner Seas Project microplastic laundry filters (one of fourteen UK installations). On-site wind turbine (rated 50 kW, runs four months a year). Biomass boiler (running on estate-cropped willow). 50 acres of new mixed broadleaf woodland planted since 2019. Cornish Black Bee restoration partnership. EV charging fleet across central car park. 87% staff hired within 10 miles. CoaST Gold accredited.

*The Scarlet Hotel* (Mawgan Porth). Multi-award-winning eco-hotel; B Corp certified. Reed-bed pool, on-site PV, full greywater system. Single-supplier provenance on the spa products.

*Bedruthan Hotel* (Mawgan Porth). Sister-hotel to The Scarlet. B Corp certified. Carbon-neutral operations since 2022. Strong on family eco-education.

*Cabilla Cornwall* (Bodmin Moor). Restoration project — 250 acres of moorland rewilded since 2019. Two-bedroom eco-cabins, off-grid. Booking for wellness retreats only.

*Trevibban Mill Vineyard* (Padstow area). Organic and biodynamic Cornish vineyard. Single-estate wines, no transport miles beyond the bottle.

*Camel Valley Vineyard* (Nanstallon, Bodmin). Family-run Cornish vineyard, organic, world-class sparkling wines, day visits.

*Greenbank Hotel* (Falmouth). Carbon-neutral hotel, harbourside, working with a marine biology partner on Falmouth Bay restoration.

*Watergate Bay Hotel* (Newquay). PV-powered, beach-cleaning programme, locally sourced food, family-orientated rather than retreat-orientated.

*Padstow Townhouse* (Padstow). Small boutique, B Corp candidate, deep local supply chain, Stein-family integrated.

*The Pig at Harlyn Bay* (near Padstow). Famously farm-to-fork; everything within a 25-mile radius. Beautiful interior; expensive.

That's the meaningful list. About a dozen others claim the label without the data; ask the questions below and you'll see why.

**The five questions to ask any operator.** Use these. The answers are revealing.

*"What's your annual carbon footprint per guest-night, and where is it published?"* If they don't measure it, it is not being managed.

*"Where does your spa water go after the treatment?"* Microplastics from spa products are a real issue. The serious operators have filtration or full greywater.

*"What percentage of your food comes from within 25 miles?"* The serious operators are 50%+. Most are 10-15%.

*"What is your tipping policy?"* Sustainability includes staff. Tronc systems and living-wage policies matter.

*"Who audits your sustainability claims?"* The right answer is a name — CoaST, B Corp, ISO 14001, Carbon Trust. The wrong answer is "we do it internally."

**What about offsetting?** Carbon offsetting is real, but it is a third-best option after reducing emissions and switching to renewables. Operators who lead with offsetting (rather than reduction) are usually compensating for not doing the harder work. Real offsetting is also auditable — Gold Standard or Verra-certified projects only. "We plant a tree per guest" without verification is greenwashing.

**On the Cleaner Seas Project specifically.** This is worth a paragraph because almost nobody is doing it. Cornish washing machines — particularly hotel and holiday laundries — are one of the largest single sources of microplastic shed into the Atlantic. The Cleaner Seas Project, founded in Bude in 2018, manufactures a filter that retrofits onto industrial laundry machines and captures shed microfibres before they reach the sewer. Each filter prevents an estimated 14 million microfibre particles per machine per year from entering the ocean. There are fourteen installations in the UK as of May 2026. Three are in Cornwall. One is on our estate. The cost of the filter is borne by the operator — there is no consumer-facing premium. Most operators have simply not chosen to do it.

**On the wind turbine.** Whalesborough's 50 kW wind turbine sits on the eastern boundary of the estate at Neetfield, generates roughly 90,000 kWh per year (about a quarter of the resort's annual demand), and was installed in 2018. We chose 50 kW because anything larger required full planning and visual impact assessment for the SWCP corridor. The remainder of our electrical demand is offset via a Cornwall-only renewable supply contract (Octopus Tracker, audited monthly). On-site PV is in planning for the lower outbuildings.

**On the woodland.** Since 2019 we have planted 50 acres (about 25,000 saplings) of mixed broadleaf woodland across the eastern Neetfield parcel. Oak, beech, hornbeam, hazel, rowan, and field maple, with a thirty-year management plan written by a CoaST-affiliated forester. The carbon sequestration is calculable (rough modelling: 600 tonnes CO2 over 50 years), but the more interesting benefit is the wildlife corridor — the woodland connects two previously isolated patches of ancient woodland for the first time in 100 years. Bird-count surveys (annual) show pied flycatcher and lesser-spotted woodpecker returning.

**The honest disclosure.** We are not perfectly sustainable and no operator is. Our laundry filters do not catch all microplastics. Our turbine is small. Our biomass boiler still emits particulates. Our guests still arrive overwhelmingly by car (90% in 2025). We are working on each. The point is not perfection — the point is measurement, transparency, and progress. We will publish our 2026 sustainability report at the end of the season, with the data audited by CoaST, and we will publish the parts that look bad alongside the parts that look good. That is the honest standard.

If you are choosing a Cornish operator on sustainability grounds: ask the five questions above. Verify the certifications. Read the published reports. The marketing language is now everywhere — the actual work is rarer, but it is here, and Whalesborough is one of the places it is being done.
