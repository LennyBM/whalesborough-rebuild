The shape of what "working" means has changed — wind turbine instead of horse-plough, market garden instead of dairy, holiday cottages instead of farmhouse rooms — but the principle is the same. ### Intro copy (≈200 words)

We commissioned this dashboard for one reason: a sustainability claim ought to be checkable on a Tuesday afternoon, not buried in a PDF that nobody reads.

The tiles below are connected to live or near-live data feeds. The wind turbine talks to us through its inverter; the Cleaner Seas filters report their wash counts; the EV chargers log their kilowatt-hours; the bee count and the food-miles are entered manually by named members of the team, each with a "last reported on" date so you can see whether the number is fresh.

Where a tile is annual rather than live — for example, tree counts — we tell you that, and we tell you when the next survey is due.

If a number looks too round, it probably is. Tap any tile to see how it was measured, by whom, and when it was last verified. If something has gone stale, please email `[VERIFY sustainability contact email]` and we'll chase it.

### 3.1 `/about/sustainability/microplastic-protection`

**Page title:** Microplastic protection — Cleaner Seas filters at Whalesborough
**Meta description:** Every cottage washing machine has a Cleaner Seas filter. Up to 700,000 microfibres prevented per wash. The full story, with methodology.

#### Hero

**Eyebrow:** Cleaner Seas at Whalesborough
**H1:** The filter behind every washing machine
**Subhead:** A small grey cartridge in a utility cupboard. A measurable difference offshore.

#### Lede (≈90 words)

Most washing machines shed microscopic plastic fibres into the wastewater system every time a synthetic fleece, a quick-dry towel or a polyester shirt goes in. Those fibres are too small for water-treatment plants to catch and too small to be visible to the human eye. They wash out to sea and they stay there. In 2022 we installed Cleaner Seas Group's INDIKON commercial filter on the washing machine in every cottage on the estate `[VERIFY 2022 install year and cottage count]`. We were the first holiday resort in the UK to do so. `[VERIFY whether this still holds in May 2026 — if not, soften to "early adopter, first in 2022"]`

#### How the filter works (≈200 words)

The INDIKON unit sits in-line between the washing machine drain and the household drain. Every time the machine empties, the water passes through a mesh cartridge that captures fibres down to roughly 50 microns — small enough to retain the fibres shed by synthetic fabrics, large enough not to choke under the flow.

Cleaner Seas Group, the Cornwall-based engineering company that developed INDIKON, has published peer-reviewed data with the University of Portsmouth on the volume of microfibres released per wash. The figure most often quoted — and the one we use on our dashboard — is **up to 700,000 microfibres per wash** for a domestic-scale load of synthetic textiles `[VERIFY current published source and link]`.

Cartridges are replaced on a schedule by `[VERIFY service technician arrangement — Cleaner Seas direct, or in-house]`. The replaced cartridges are returned to Cleaner Seas, where the captured fibre is processed and reused in recycled products — packaging, insulation, downstream textiles — closing the loop properly rather than landfilling the waste.

If you'd like to see the unit in your cottage, it's behind the inspection panel under the worktop next to the machine. We're not hiding it. It's there to be opened, photographed, and asked about.

#### What we count on the dashboard (≈150 words)

The dashboard tile counts washes, not fibres directly. We multiply by 700,000 — the upper figure published by Cleaner Seas — only because it is the figure that's actually been measured. We will not invent a number. If you see a tile claiming a more conservative figure, that's because we've switched to a load-weighted average from the next published Cleaner Seas study `[VERIFY update timing]`.

We also report two derived numbers on the impact card we send after your stay: the washes the cottage ran during your visit, and the implied microfibre count those washes prevented entering the sea. The default assumption — if the cottage's wash count is not logged for any reason — is **two loads per stay**, which is the average we have measured over `[VERIFY survey period]` of usage data across the resort.

#### Limits and honest caveats (≈120 words)

A filter is not a perfect device. It does not retain fibres smaller than its mesh. It only catches what washes through the machine — it does not catch fibres that shed elsewhere, including, for example, when a synthetic fleece is hung outside to dry and the wind takes its fibres. We are also not solving the upstream problem: synthetic textiles still exist, and the best filter in the world is downstream of bad fabric choices.

What the filter does do is measurably reduce the load entering the wastewater system from this estate. That is a real intervention. It is not, on its own, a "we have solved microfibre pollution" claim, and we have never made one.

#### How it actually works (≈200 words)

### 3.3 `/about/sustainability/bees`

**Page title:** 15,000 Cornish Black Bees — Whalesborough
**Meta description:** The native dark bee, `Apis mellifera mellifera`, lives across our hives. The beekeeping programme, the harvest, the policy.

#### Hero

**Eyebrow:** The estate's pollinators
**H1:** 15,000 native bees, the right way around
**Subhead:** We keep the Cornish Black Bee — the native sub-species — because it's adapted to this weather, this rainfall, this hedgerow. The imported Italian bee is more productive and less suited.

#### Lede (≈110 words)

Honeybees are not native to Cornwall as a generic category — they are native as a *specific* sub-species, `Apis mellifera mellifera`, often called the European Dark Bee or, locally, the Cornish Black Bee. It evolved in a maritime climate, copes with damp summers, forages in lower temperatures, overwinters frugally on a smaller honey store, and is generally a calmer bee than the imported Italian varieties bred for industrial pollination. There are around 15,000 of these bees living across our hives at present `[VERIFY current hive and bee count — 15,000 is a rounded "approximately" figure; methodology is bees-per-hive × hive count]`. They are looked after by `[VERIFY beekeeper name]`.

#### Why the Cornish Black Bee, not a more productive imported strain (≈190 words)

The honest answer: it produces less honey per hive than an Italian strain would, in a good year. In a bad year — wet July, cold August, a late spring — it produces more honey than the Italians, because it doesn't burn through its store as quickly and it'll forage in conditions that keep the imports inside. The Italian bee is bred for the Mediterranean. The Cornish Black is bred for here.

There's also a deeper argument. The Italian honeybee is so widely kept in the UK that the gene pool of the native dark bee is now small and locally vulnerable. Keeping `mellifera mellifera` is, in a small way, part of the conservation work for the sub-species. The Bee Improvement and Bee Breeders Association `[VERIFY whether we're a member]` and `[VERIFY local Cornwall Black Bee group]` work to maintain native-strain breeding lines, and we source queens through that network.

We mention this because guests sometimes ask why our honey runs out by autumn. The answer is that we keep slightly fewer, slightly less productive, slightly more locally appropriate bees on purpose.

#### The beekeeping year (≈250 words)

**March–April:** the first spring inspections. The colony has overwintered on its own honey stores. We check brood pattern, queen viability, disease (specifically varroa load — every responsible UK beekeeper monitors this, year-round). Any colony that has died over winter is recorded; the equipment is sterilised before re-use.

**May–June:** the main build-up. Forage is dandelion, hawthorn, sycamore, then early hedgerow flowers. We add brood frames and supers as needed. Late May to early June is when swarming pressure peaks — and we manage rather than prevent it, with controlled splits.

**July:** the first honey harvest, if the spring has been kind. This is the lightest, palest honey of the year — a clear, almost greenish gold from hawthorn-and-bramble nectar.

**August:** the bramble flow. The bees are working the deep hedges. Heat is the enemy this month — both for the colony and for the beekeeper in a suit.

**September:** the second honey harvest. Darker, richer, more amber. Heather honey is rare here (we're slightly too far from the moor), but a good late summer brings some ling.

**October:** winter prep. The colony reduces, the queen stops laying. We treat for varroa and reduce hive entrances to keep wasps and field mice out.

**November–February:** the bees cluster in the hive, slowly burning their honey reserve. We don't open the hives unless absolutely necessary.

The harvest in jars is sold in the Estate Shop. The yield in 2024 was `[VERIFY 2024 figure in kg or jars]`.

#### Beekeeper for a Day (≈80 words)

We run a four-hour immersive programme on selected Saturdays from May to September. £125 per person, cap of four people per session, ages 16 and over `[VERIFY current price and capacity]`. You suit up, inspect a working hive with the beekeeper, observe a frame extraction, and take a numbered jar of honey home. It is the only honey we don't sell — those jars are reserved for participants. Bookable through the Whalesborough app.
